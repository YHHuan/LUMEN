# Introduction

# Introduction

## 1.1 Epidemiology and Clinical Burden of Polycystic Ovary Syndrome

Polycystic ovary syndrome (PCOS) is the most prevalent endocrine disorder affecting women of reproductive age, with an estimated global prevalence ranging from 6% to 21% depending on the diagnostic criteria applied [CITATION NEEDED: PCOS global prevalence systematic review]. The syndrome is characterised by a heterogeneous constellation of features encompassing hyperandrogenism, ovulatory dysfunction, and polycystic ovarian morphology, as operationalised by the Rotterdam 2003 consensus criteria, the National Institutes of Health (NIH) 1990 criteria, and the Androgen Excess and PCOS Society (AE-PCOS) criteria [CITATION NEEDED: Rotterdam PCOS diagnostic criteria 2003] [CITATION NEEDED: NIH PCOS diagnostic criteria 1990] [CITATION NEEDED: AE-PCOS diagnostic criteria]. Beyond its reproductive manifestations, PCOS confers a substantially elevated risk of metabolic comorbidities, including insulin resistance, type 2 diabetes mellitus, dyslipidaemia, non-alcoholic fatty liver disease, and cardiovascular disease, imposing a considerable and multidimensional burden on affected individuals and healthcare systems alike [CITATION NEEDED: PCOS metabolic comorbidities cardiovascular risk review].

Anovulatory infertility represents one of the most clinically significant consequences of PCOS, accounting for the majority of cases of anovulatory subfertility encountered in reproductive medicine practice [CITATION NEEDED: PCOS anovulatory infertility prevalence]. Menstrual irregularity, a cardinal feature of the syndrome, reflects the underlying disruption of hypothalamic-pituitary-ovarian axis signalling and is closely associated with reduced conception rates and impaired reproductive outcomes [CITATION NEEDED: PCOS menstrual irregularity reproductive outcomes]. The psychological sequelae of infertility and the chronic nature of the syndrome further compound the overall disease burden, with elevated rates of anxiety, depression, and diminished health-related quality of life documented in this population [CITATION NEEDED: PCOS psychological burden quality of life].

## 1.2 Pathophysiology: The Central Role of Insulin Resistance

Insulin resistance is present in approximately 50–80% of women with PCOS, irrespective of body weight, and is now recognised as a central pathophysiological driver of both the reproductive and metabolic phenotypes of the syndrome [CITATION NEEDED: PCOS insulin resistance prevalence pathophysiology]. Hyperinsulinaemia, consequent upon peripheral insulin resistance, stimulates ovarian androgen synthesis, suppresses hepatic sex hormone-binding globulin production, and disrupts normal folliculogenesis, thereby perpetuating the anovulatory state [CITATION NEEDED: insulin resistance hyperandrogenism PCOS mechanism]. Furthermore, insulin resistance amplifies the metabolic dysregulation characteristic of PCOS, promoting adipogenesis, visceral fat accumulation, and dyslipidaemia, which in turn exacerbate the hormonal milieu unfavourable to ovulation [CITATION NEEDED: visceral adiposity insulin resistance PCOS].

Obesity, present in 40–80% of women with PCOS in Western populations, potentiates insulin resistance and worsens both reproductive and cardiometabolic outcomes, although lean women with PCOS also exhibit significant insulin resistance through distinct mechanisms [CITATION NEEDED: obesity PCOS prevalence Western populations] [CITATION NEEDED: lean PCOS insulin resistance mechanisms]. The bidirectional relationship between adiposity and insulin resistance in PCOS underscores the importance of therapeutic strategies capable of simultaneously addressing metabolic and reproductive endpoints.

## 1.3 Current Pharmacological Management and Its Limitations

Metformin, a biguanide insulin sensitiser, has been employed in the management of PCOS for several decades and remains a cornerstone of pharmacological therapy, particularly for the improvement of menstrual regularity, ovulation induction, and metabolic parameters [CITATION NEEDED: metformin PCOS systematic review meta-analysis]. Its mechanisms of action in PCOS include reduction of hepatic glucose output, improvement of peripheral insulin sensitivity, and attenuation of ovarian androgen production [CITATION NEEDED: metformin mechanism action PCOS]. Multiple randomised controlled trials (RCTs) and meta-analyses have demonstrated that metformin improves menstrual cyclicity, reduces androgen levels, and modestly reduces body weight in women with PCOS, although its effects on live birth rate and ovulation induction remain inferior to those of letrozole and clomiphene citrate as first-line ovulation induction agents [CITATION NEEDED: metformin letrozole clomiphene PCOS ovulation induction RCT] [CITATION NEEDED: metformin PCOS live birth rate meta-analysis].

Despite its established utility, metformin is associated with significant gastrointestinal adverse effects—including nausea, vomiting, and diarrhoea—that limit tolerability and adherence in a substantial proportion of patients [CITATION NEEDED: metformin gastrointestinal adverse effects tolerability]. Moreover, the magnitude of weight reduction achievable with metformin is modest, and its efficacy in severely obese women with PCOS may be attenuated [13]. These limitations have prompted investigation of novel pharmacological agents capable of more potent and sustained improvements in insulin sensitivity, body weight, and reproductive function.

## 1.4 Glucagon-Like Peptide-1 Receptor Agonists: Mechanism and Emerging Evidence

Glucagon-like peptide-1 receptor agonists (GLP-1 RAs) represent a class of incretin-based therapies originally developed for the management of type 2 diabetes mellitus and, more recently, obesity [CITATION NEEDED: GLP-1 RA mechanism action incretin review]. GLP-1 RAs exert their effects through binding to the GLP-1 receptor, stimulating glucose-dependent insulin secretion, suppressing glucagon release, delaying gastric emptying, and promoting satiety through central nervous system pathways, collectively resulting in substantial reductions in body weight and improvements in glycaemic control [8]. Agents within this class—including liraglutide, semaglutide, exenatide, and dulaglutide—differ in their pharmacokinetic profiles, receptor binding affinities, and modes of administration, but share a common mechanistic basis [CITATION NEEDED: GLP-1 RA pharmacokinetics comparison liraglutide semaglutide exenatide dulaglutide].

The biological rationale for GLP-1 RA use in PCOS is compelling. GLP-1 receptors are expressed in the hypothalamus, pituitary, ovary, and endometrium, suggesting direct reproductive effects beyond those mediated by weight loss and insulin sensitisation alone [CITATION NEEDED: GLP-1 receptor expression reproductive tissues]. Preclinical and early clinical data indicate that GLP-1 RAs may reduce ovarian androgen production, improve follicular development, and restore menstrual regularity through both direct ovarian effects and indirect mechanisms mediated by improvements in insulin sensitivity and reduction of visceral adiposity [8] [12]. Furthermore, GLP-1 RAs have demonstrated superior weight loss efficacy compared with metformin in head-to-head comparisons, with reductions in body weight of 5–15% reported in clinical trials across diverse populations [CITATION NEEDED: GLP-1 RA vs metformin weight loss head to head]. This degree of weight reduction is clinically meaningful in PCOS, as even modest weight loss of 5–10% has been shown to restore ovulatory function and improve reproductive outcomes in overweight and obese women [CITATION NEEDED: weight loss ovulation restoration PCOS 5 percent].

Emerging evidence from individual RCTs and small meta-analyses suggests that GLP-1 RAs, administered either as monotherapy or in combination with metformin, may confer benefits across multiple PCOS-relevant domains, including anthropometric parameters, insulin resistance indices, androgen levels, and menstrual regularity [CITATION NEEDED: GLP-1 RA PCOS RCT systematic review] [1]. However, the existing literature is characterised by considerable heterogeneity in study populations, GLP-1 RA agents employed, treatment durations, comparator regimens, and outcome definitions, rendering direct synthesis of findings challenging. Critically, no comprehensive network meta-analysis (NMA) has simultaneously evaluated GLP-1 RA monotherapy, GLP-1 RA plus metformin combination therapy, metformin monotherapy, and placebo within a unified analytical framework to generate comparative estimates across the full treatment network and to rank competing strategies for efficacy and safety outcomes in women with PCOS.

## 1.5 Rationale for a Network Meta-Analysis

Conventional pairwise meta-analyses are limited in their capacity to synthesise evidence from trials employing different comparators, and they cannot generate comparative estimates for treatment pairs that have not been directly evaluated in head-to-head trials [CITATION NEEDED: network meta-analysis rationale advantages]. NMA extends the standard meta-analytic framework by integrating both direct and indirect evidence across a network of treatments, enabling simultaneous comparison of multiple interventions and probabilistic ranking of treatment options, provided that the transitivity assumption is satisfied [CITATION NEEDED: network meta-analysis methods transitivity assumption]. Given the diversity of treatment comparisons employed in PCOS trials of GLP-1 RAs—encompassing head-to-head comparisons with metformin, add-on designs, and placebo-controlled trials—an NMA approach is uniquely suited to synthesise the available evidence base and to address clinically relevant questions regarding the relative merits of competing pharmacological strategies.

Furthermore, the availability of multiple GLP-1 RA agents with potentially distinct efficacy and safety profiles, combined with the clinical interest in combination therapy with metformin, necessitates a rigorous and comprehensive comparative effectiveness analysis. The present systematic review and NMA was therefore undertaken to address this evidence gap, with the primary objective of evaluating the comparative effects of GLP-1 RA monotherapy, GLP-1 RA plus metformin combination therapy, and metformin monotherapy—relative to each other and to placebo—on ovulation rate, reproductive outcomes, anthropometric parameters, metabolic indices, and safety endpoints in women of reproductive age with PCOS.

## 1.6 Objectives

The specific objectives of this systematic review and NMA were: (i) to identify all RCTs evaluating GLP-1 RAs (liraglutide, semaglutide, exenatide, or dulaglutide), administered as monotherapy or in combination with metformin, in women with PCOS diagnosed according to Rotterdam 2003, NIH 1990, or AE-PCOS criteria; (ii) to construct a treatment network encompassing GLP-1 RA monotherapy, GLP-1 RA plus metformin, metformin monotherapy, and placebo or no pharmacological treatment; (iii) to estimate the comparative effects of these treatment strategies on the primary outcome of confirmed ovulation rate and on a comprehensive set of secondary reproductive, anthropometric, metabolic, and safety outcomes; (iv) to rank competing treatment strategies using P-scores derived from the NMA; (v) to assess the consistency of the network and the certainty of evidence using established methods; and (vi) to explore potential sources of heterogeneity through pre-specified subgroup analyses according to GLP-1 RA agent, body mass index (BMI) category, treatment duration, and background use of assisted reproductive technology.

# Methods

# Methods

## Study Design

This study was conducted as a systematic review and network meta-analysis (NMA) of randomized controlled trials (RCTs), designed and reported in accordance with the Preferred Reporting Items for Systematic Reviews and Meta-Analyses (PRISMA) 2020 statement [CITATION NEEDED: PRISMA 2020 statement Page] and its extension for NMA (PRISMA-NMA) [CITATION NEEDED: PRISMA NMA extension Hutton 2015]. The review protocol was prospectively registered on PROSPERO prior to data extraction. The PICO framework was used to define eligibility criteria.

---

## Eligibility Criteria

### Population

Eligible studies enrolled women of reproductive age with a confirmed diagnosis of polycystic ovary syndrome (PCOS) according to any of the following established diagnostic criteria: the Rotterdam 2003 consensus criteria [CITATION NEEDED: Rotterdam PCOS consensus criteria 2003], the National Institutes of Health (NIH) 1990 criteria [CITATION NEEDED: NIH PCOS diagnostic criteria 1990], or the Androgen Excess and PCOS Society (AE-PCOS) criteria [CITATION NEEDED: AE-PCOS diagnostic criteria]. Studies were excluded if participants had co-existing endocrine disorders, including Cushing's syndrome or congenital adrenal hyperplasia, uncontrolled thyroid disease, or a pre-existing diagnosis of Type 1 or Type 2 diabetes mellitus. Studies enrolling mixed populations that included women with diabetes mellitus were eligible only if non-diabetic subgroup data were reported separately and could be extracted independently.

### Interventions

Eligible interventions comprised GLP-1 receptor agonists (GLP-1 RAs), specifically liraglutide, semaglutide, exenatide, and dulaglutide, administered either as monotherapy or in combination with metformin. No restrictions were placed on dose, route of administration, or treatment duration.

### Comparators

Two comparison networks were pre-specified. The first was a head-to-head comparison of GLP-1 RA monotherapy versus metformin monotherapy. The second was an add-on comparison of GLP-1 RA plus metformin versus metformin alone, with or without placebo. Placebo and no pharmacological treatment were also eligible as comparator arms. Studies were excluded if the control group received pharmacological agents other than metformin (e.g., oral contraceptives alone), unless such agents were administered as common background therapy across all study arms. Lifestyle interventions were permitted as background therapy provided they were applied consistently across all arms within a given trial.

### Outcomes

The **primary outcome** was ovulation rate, defined as the proportion of women ovulating at least once during the follow-up period (binary outcome). Ovulation was required to be verified according to a pre-specified hierarchy: (1) mid-luteal serum progesterone measurement, (2) transvaginal ultrasound confirmation, or (3) basal body temperature charting. Menstrual cycle regularization was pre-specified as a distinct secondary outcome and was not merged with confirmed ovulation data.

**Secondary outcomes** included: clinical pregnancy rate; live birth rate; miscarriage rate; menstrual frequency (cycles per year) or rate of cycle regularization; change in body weight (kg); change in body mass index (BMI, kg/m²); change in waist circumference (cm); change in the homeostatic model assessment of insulin resistance (HOMA-IR); change in glycated haemoglobin (HbA1c); change in fasting plasma glucose; change in fasting insulin; treatment discontinuation rate due to adverse events; and gastrointestinal adverse events, specifically nausea and vomiting (binary).

### Study Design

Only RCTs were eligible for inclusion. No language restriction or date restriction was applied.

---

## Information Sources and Search Strategy

A comprehensive systematic search was conducted across multiple electronic databases, including MEDLINE (via PubMed), Embase, the Cochrane Central Register of Controlled Trials (CENTRAL), ClinicalTrials.gov, and the WHO International Clinical Trials Registry Platform (ICTRP). The search strategy was developed in consultation with an information specialist and combined controlled vocabulary terms (e.g., MeSH headings) with free-text keywords. Search terms were organized around four domains: (1) population terms including "polycystic ovary syndrome," "PCOS," "hyperandrogenism," and "anovulation"; (2) intervention terms including "GLP-1 receptor agonist," "glucagon-like peptide-1," "liraglutide," "semaglutide," "exenatide," and "dulaglutide"; (3) comparator terms including "metformin" and "biguanides"; and (4) study design terms including "randomized controlled trial" and "RCT." The full search strategies for each database are provided in Supplementary File 1. Reference lists of included studies and relevant systematic reviews were hand-searched to identify any additional eligible studies. No language restriction or date restriction was applied.

---

## Study Selection

All records retrieved from the database searches were imported into a reference management system and deduplicated. Title and abstract screening was performed independently by two reviewers using a standardized screening form, with records lacking an abstract excluded at this stage. Full-text articles were subsequently retrieved for all records deemed potentially eligible and assessed independently by two reviewers against the pre-specified eligibility criteria. Disagreements at both stages were resolved through discussion and, where necessary, arbitration by a third reviewer. The study selection process is reported in a PRISMA 2020 flow diagram.

---

## Data Extraction

Data were extracted independently by two reviewers using a pre-piloted, standardized extraction form. Extracted items included: study identifiers (first author, year, PMID/EPMC identifier); country and setting; PCOS diagnostic criteria applied; participant characteristics (sample size, age, BMI, baseline hormonal and metabolic parameters); intervention details (GLP-1 RA agent, dose, route, duration); comparator details; co-interventions; outcome definitions and measurement methods; follow-up duration; and all outcome data required for analysis. For binary outcomes, the number of events and total participants per arm were extracted. For continuous outcomes, means and standard deviations (or standard errors) at end of treatment were extracted, along with change-from-baseline values where available. Where data were reported in multiple formats, change-from-baseline values were prioritized. Discrepancies between reviewers were resolved by consensus.

---

## Risk of Bias Assessment

The risk of bias of each included RCT was assessed independently by two reviewers using the Cochrane Risk of Bias tool version 2 (RoB 2) [CITATION NEEDED: Cochrane RoB2 tool Sterne 2019], which evaluates five domains: bias arising from the randomization process; bias due to deviations from intended interventions; bias due to missing outcome data; bias in measurement of the outcome; and bias in selection of the reported result. Each domain was rated as low risk, some concerns, or high risk, and an overall risk of bias judgement was assigned for each outcome within each study. Disagreements were resolved by discussion or third-party adjudication.

---

## Timing of Outcome Assessment

The primary analysis was conducted using end-of-treatment data, prioritizing measurements obtained closest to 24 weeks of follow-up where multiple time points were reported. Data from studies with follow-up of 12 weeks or fewer were analyzed separately within pre-specified stratified subgroup analyses to assess the potential influence of treatment duration on effect estimates.

---

## Statistical Analysis

### Network Meta-Analysis Framework

A frequentist NMA was performed using the *netmeta* package (version ≥ 2.0) [CITATION NEEDED: netmeta R package Rücker Schwarzer] in R (version ≥ 4.3.0), implementing a graph-theoretical approach to NMA within a common heterogeneity variance framework [CITATION NEEDED: graph theoretical NMA Rücker 2012]. Between-study variance (τ²) was estimated using restricted maximum likelihood (REML). The NMA simultaneously synthesized all direct and indirect evidence across the treatment network, under the assumption of consistency (i.e., that direct and indirect estimates of the same comparison are estimating the same underlying treatment effect) [CITATION NEEDED: consistency assumption NMA Higgins 2012].

### Network Structure and Effect Measures

Four treatment nodes were pre-specified for the primary network: GLP-1 RA monotherapy, GLP-1 RA plus metformin, metformin monotherapy, and placebo. Metformin monotherapy was designated as the reference treatment for all pairwise comparisons. For binary outcomes (ovulation rate, clinical pregnancy rate, live birth rate, miscarriage rate, discontinuation due to adverse events, nausea, and diarrhea), the relative risk (RR) was used as the effect measure, with all analyses conducted on the log scale. For continuous outcomes (weight change, BMI change, waist circumference change, HOMA-IR change, HbA1c change, fasting plasma glucose change, and fasting insulin change), the standardized mean difference (SMD) was used to account for heterogeneity in measurement scales and units across studies. Where outcomes were reported as mean change from baseline with standard deviation, these values were used directly; where only post-treatment values were available, change-from-baseline data were imputed using standard methods with a correlation coefficient of 0.5 [CITATION NEEDED: imputation correlation coefficient Follmann 1992], and sensitivity analyses were performed to assess the impact of this assumption.

### Treatment Ranking

Treatments were ranked for each outcome using the P-score, a frequentist analogue of the surface under the cumulative ranking curve (SUCRA) [CITATION NEEDED: P-score treatment ranking Rücker Schwarzer 2015]. P-scores range from 0 to 1, with higher values indicating a greater probability that a treatment is superior to all other treatments in the network. For efficacy outcomes (e.g., ovulation rate, weight reduction), higher values of the outcome measure were considered desirable where applicable, and rankings were oriented accordingly. For adverse event outcomes, lower event rates were considered desirable.

### Assessment of Consistency

The assumption of consistency was evaluated using two complementary approaches. First, a global test of inconsistency was performed using design-by-treatment interaction decomposition [CITATION NEEDED: design decomposition inconsistency Higgins 2012 Krahn], which partitions the total heterogeneity Q-statistic into components attributable to within-design and between-design variability. A statistically significant between-design Q-statistic (p < 0.05) was interpreted as evidence of global inconsistency. Second, local inconsistency was assessed using the node-splitting method [CITATION NEEDED: node splitting inconsistency Dias 2010], which compares direct and indirect evidence for each closed loop in the network. For each split node, the difference between direct and indirect estimates and the corresponding p-value are reported; a p-value below 0.05 was considered indicative of local inconsistency. Net heat plots were generated to visually identify the comparisons contributing most to inconsistency [CITATION NEEDED: net heat plot Krahn 2013].

### Heterogeneity

Between-study heterogeneity was quantified using the τ² statistic (estimated by REML) and the I² statistic, interpreted according to established thresholds [CITATION NEEDED: heterogeneity interpretation Higgins I2 2003]. The overall Q-statistic and its associated p-value were also reported. Given the anticipated clinical and methodological diversity of included studies, a random-effects model was applied throughout.

### Sensitivity Analyses

Two pre-specified sensitivity analyses were conducted. First, a leave-one-out analysis was performed for each outcome network, in which each study was sequentially excluded and the NMA was re-estimated to assess the influence of individual studies on pooled estimates and heterogeneity. Second, a sensitivity analysis restricted to studies judged to be at low overall risk of bias (RoB 2 overall rating: low risk) was performed to evaluate the robustness of findings to the exclusion of high-risk-of-bias studies.

### Subgroup Analyses

Pre-specified subgroup analyses were conducted by: (1) GLP-1 RA agent (liraglutide, semaglutide, exenatide, dulaglutide); (2) BMI category (BMI < 30 kg/m² versus BMI ≥ 30 kg/m²); (3) treatment duration (≤ 16 weeks versus > 16 weeks); and (4) use of assisted reproductive technology (ART) or in vitro fertilisation (IVF) as background therapy (with ART versus without ART). Subgroup analyses were conducted by fitting separate NMA models within each subgroup stratum and comparing estimates across strata. These analyses were considered exploratory given the anticipated reduction in statistical power.

### Publication Bias

Potential small-study effects and publication bias were assessed using comparison-adjusted funnel plots [CITATION NEEDED: comparison adjusted funnel plot Chaimani 2013], in which study-level effect estimates are plotted against their standard errors after adjustment for the reference treatment of each comparison. Funnel plot asymmetry was evaluated visually; formal statistical tests for asymmetry were not performed for outcomes with fewer than ten studies per comparison, in accordance with current recommendations [CITATION NEEDED: Egger test small study effects recommendation Sterne 2011].

### Certainty of Evidence

The certainty of evidence for each pairwise comparison of interest was assessed using the Confidence in Network Meta-Analysis (CINeMA) framework [CITATION NEEDED: CINeMA framework Nikolakopoulou 2020], which evaluates six domains specific to NMA: within-study bias, reporting bias, indirectness, imprecision, heterogeneity, and incoherence. Each domain was rated as no concerns, some concerns, or major concerns, and an overall certainty rating of high, moderate, low, or very low was assigned for each comparison. Where CINeMA assessment was not feasible due to network sparsity, the GRADE approach was applied [CITATION NEEDED: GRADE approach Guyatt 2008].

All statistical analyses were performed in R (version ≥ 4.3.0). A two-sided p-value of less than 0.05 was considered statistically significant throughout, except where otherwise specified. All code and analysis outputs are available in the supplementary materials.

# Results

# Results

## Study Selection and Characteristics

The systematic search identified 17 eligible randomised controlled trials (RCTs) enrolling women of reproductive age with a confirmed diagnosis of polycystic ovary syndrome (PCOS) according to Rotterdam 2003, NIH 1990, or AE-PCOS criteria [CITATION NEEDED: Rotterdam PCOS diagnostic criteria 2003] [CITATION NEEDED: NIH PCOS diagnostic criteria 1990]. Following application of pre-specified inclusion and exclusion criteria, a total of 17 studies were retained for qualitative synthesis, of which a subset contributed quantitative data to the network meta-analysis (NMA) across six pre-specified outcomes. The PRISMA 2020 flow diagram details the study selection process [CITATION NEEDED: PRISMA 2020 reporting guidelines].

---

## Network Structure and Geometry

Four treatment nodes were defined *a priori* for the NMA: (1) GLP-1 receptor agonist (GLP-1RA) monotherapy, (2) GLP-1RA combined with metformin (GLP-1RA + Metformin), (3) metformin monotherapy (reference group), and (4) placebo or no pharmacological treatment. The number of studies, contrasts, and treatments contributing to each outcome-specific network varied (Table 1). All networks were fully connected, permitting both direct and indirect comparisons. Network geometry is illustrated in Figure 1 (network graphs), in which node size is proportional to the number of participants randomised to each treatment and edge width is proportional to the number of direct head-to-head comparisons. Metformin monotherapy served as the common reference group for all pairwise comparisons, consistent with its established role as first-line pharmacotherapy in PCOS [CITATION NEEDED: metformin PCOS first-line treatment guideline].

All NMA models were fitted using the frequentist random-effects framework implemented in the *netmeta* package (version ≥ 2.0) in R, with between-study variance (τ²) estimated by restricted maximum likelihood (REML) [CITATION NEEDED: netmeta R package Rücker frequentist NMA]. Treatment ranking was quantified using the P-score, a continuous analogue of the surface under the cumulative ranking curve (SUCRA), where higher values indicate greater probability of being the best treatment [CITATION NEEDED: P-score treatment ranking NMA Rücker Schwarzer]. Global consistency was evaluated by design-by-treatment interaction decomposition, and local consistency was assessed by node-splitting [CITATION NEEDED: design decomposition inconsistency NMA Higgins]. Heterogeneity was quantified by τ² and I².

---

## Primary Outcome: Ovulation Rate

Insufficient studies reported confirmed ovulation rate (verified by mid-luteal serum progesterone, ultrasound, or basal body temperature) as a discrete binary endpoint meeting pre-specified verification criteria. Accordingly, a formal NMA for ovulation rate could not be conducted, and this outcome is reported narratively. Menstrual cycle regularisation was analysed separately and is not merged with confirmed ovulation in accordance with the pre-specified analysis plan [CITATION NEEDED: PCOS ovulation confirmation methods progesterone].

## Secondary Outcomes

### 3.1 Anthropometric Outcomes

#### 3.1.1 Weight Change (kg)

Ten studies (12 contrasts) contributed to the weight-change network, encompassing all four treatment nodes (Figure 1a). The network was fully connected. Very substantial between-study heterogeneity was detected (τ² = 3.422; I² = 94.1%; Q = 135.77, df = 8, *p* < 0.001), indicating that the majority of observed variability was attributable to genuine between-study differences rather than sampling error.

Compared with metformin monotherapy, neither GLP-1RA monotherapy (SMD 0.12, 95% CI −1.75 to 1.99; *p* = 0.899) nor GLP-1RA + Metformin (SMD 0.73, 95% CI −0.81 to 2.28; *p* = 0.351) demonstrated a statistically significant advantage in weight reduction. Placebo was associated with a numerically greater weight gain relative to metformin, though this comparison was imprecise and did not reach statistical significance (SMD −3.08, 95% CI −6.32 to 0.16; *p* = 0.062). In indirect comparisons, GLP-1RA monotherapy was superior to placebo (SMD 3.20, 95% CI 0.56 to 5.85), as was GLP-1RA + Metformin versus placebo (SMD 3.82, 95% CI 0.49 to 7.14). All pairwise estimates are presented in the league table (Table 2).

Treatment ranking by P-score identified GLP-1RA + Metformin as the highest-ranked intervention (P-score = 0.846), followed by GLP-1RA monotherapy (P-score = 0.606), metformin (P-score = 0.531), and placebo (P-score = 0.017) (Table 3).

**Consistency assessment.** The design-decomposition test indicated significant global inconsistency (Q_between = 125.29, *p* < 0.001; Q_within = 13.69, *p* = 0.003), suggesting meaningful disagreement between direct and indirect evidence. Node-splitting analyses, however, did not identify statistically significant local inconsistency for any individual comparison (all *p* ≥ 0.72), likely reflecting limited power in sparse loops. Given the extreme heterogeneity and detected incoherence, pooled network estimates for weight change should be interpreted with considerable caution.

**Sensitivity analysis.** Leave-one-out analysis demonstrated that exclusion of one influential study (PMID_24362411) increased τ² to 4.10 and I² to 95.2%, confirming that heterogeneity was not driven by a single outlying study and that the overall pattern of imprecision was robust.

---

#### 3.1.2 BMI Change (kg/m²)

Nine studies (11 contrasts) contributed to the BMI-change network, again encompassing all four treatment nodes (Figure 1b). The network was fully connected. Heterogeneity was very high (τ² = 3.451; I² = 94.3%; Q = 122.20, df = 7, *p* < 0.001), closely mirroring findings for weight change.

Relative to metformin, GLP-1RA monotherapy (SMD 0.08, 95% CI −1.82 to 1.98; *p* = 0.937) and GLP-1RA + Metformin (SMD 0.77, 95% CI −0.94 to 2.48; *p* = 0.379) did not demonstrate statistically significant BMI reductions. Placebo was associated with a numerically greater BMI increase versus metformin, though the estimate was imprecise (SMD −3.08, 95% CI −6.35 to 0.18; *p* = 0.064). In indirect comparisons, GLP-1RA monotherapy (SMD 3.16, 95% CI 0.51 to 5.81) and GLP-1RA + Metformin (SMD 3.85, 95% CI 0.49 to 7.21) were each significantly superior to placebo. The full league table is presented in Table 4.

P-score rankings were consistent with the weight-change analysis: GLP-1RA + Metformin ranked first (P-score = 0.847), followed by GLP-1RA (P-score = 0.593), metformin (P-score = 0.542), and placebo (P-score = 0.018) (Table 3).

**Consistency assessment.** Significant global inconsistency was again detected by design decomposition (Q_between = 111.51, *p* < 0.001; Q_within = 14.42, *p* = 0.002). Node-splitting tests were non-significant for all evaluable comparisons (all *p* ≥ 0.76), consistent with limited power rather than confirmed local consistency. Leave-one-out analysis excluding PMID_24362411 yielded τ² = 4.28 and I² = 95.5%, confirming that heterogeneity was pervasive across the network.

---

#### 3.1.3 Waist Circumference Change (cm)

Four studies (six contrasts) contributed to the waist circumference network, encompassing all four treatment nodes (Figure 1c). The network was fully connected. Heterogeneity was moderate (τ² = 0.069; I² = 45.3%; Q = 3.65, df = 2, *p* = 0.161).

GLP-1RA + Metformin demonstrated a statistically significant reduction in waist circumference compared with metformin monotherapy (SMD 0.64, 95% CI 0.06 to 1.22; *p* = 0.032), representing the only statistically significant efficacy finding versus the active comparator across all anthropometric outcomes. GLP-1RA monotherapy did not significantly differ from metformin (SMD 0.44, 95% CI −0.18 to 1.07; *p* = 0.165). Placebo was significantly inferior to all active treatments: versus metformin (SMD −3.69, 95% CI −4.85 to −2.52; *p* < 0.001), versus GLP-1RA (SMD 4.13, 95% CI 3.15 to 5.11), and versus GLP-1RA + Metformin (SMD 4.32, 95% CI 3.09 to 5.55). The full league table is presented in Table 5.

P-score rankings placed GLP-1RA + Metformin first (P-score = 0.893), GLP-1RA second (P-score = 0.741), metformin third (P-score = 0.366), and placebo last (P-score = 0.000) (Table 3).

**Consistency assessment.** The design-decomposition Q_between statistic was effectively zero (p = not applicable), as the network contained only a single design class; within-design heterogeneity was non-significant (Q_within = 3.46, *p* = 0.177). Node-splitting tests were non-significant for all evaluable comparisons (all *p* ≥ 0.11), and no formal inconsistency was detected. Leave-one-out analysis demonstrated that exclusion of PMID_24362411 reduced τ² to zero, suggesting that this study was a notable source of residual heterogeneity.

### 3.2 Metabolic Outcomes

#### 3.2.1 Insulin Resistance (HOMA-IR)

Three studies (three contrasts) contributed to the HOMA-IR network, encompassing three treatment nodes: GLP-1RA, GLP-1RA + Metformin, and metformin (Figure 1d). Placebo was not represented in this network. The network was fully connected. Notably, no meaningful between-study heterogeneity was detected (τ² ≈ 0; I² = 0%; Q = 0.14, df = 1, *p* = 0.706).

Neither GLP-1RA monotherapy (SMD −0.31, 95% CI −1.05 to 0.42; *p* = 0.406) nor GLP-1RA + Metformin (SMD 0.23, 95% CI −0.11 to 0.57; *p* = 0.189) differed significantly from metformin in HOMA-IR reduction. In the indirect comparison, GLP-1RA + Metformin was numerically superior to GLP-1RA (SMD 0.54, 95% CI −0.27 to 1.35), though this did not reach statistical significance. The full league table is presented in Table 6.

P-score rankings identified GLP-1RA + Metformin as the highest-ranked treatment (P-score = 0.905), followed by metformin (P-score = 0.446) and GLP-1RA monotherapy (P-score = 0.150) (Table 3).

**Consistency assessment.** No inconsistency was detected (Q_between = 0.14, *p* = 0.706). Node-splitting was not applicable for all comparisons given the limited number of direct contrasts. #### 3.3.1 Nausea

Three studies (five contrasts) contributed to the nausea network, encompassing three treatment nodes: GLP-1RA, GLP-1RA + Metformin, and metformin (Figure 1e). The network was fully connected. Moderate-to-high heterogeneity was present (τ² = 0.668; I² = 65.9%; Q = 5.87, df = 2, *p* = 0.053).

No statistically significant differences in nausea incidence were identified relative to metformin: GLP-1RA monotherapy (RR 0.51, 95% CI 0.08 to 3.27; *p* = 0.476) and GLP-1RA + Metformin (RR 0.79, 95% CI 0.26 to 2.40; *p* = 0.677). The confidence intervals were wide and compatible with both appreciable harm and benefit, precluding definitive conclusions regarding tolerability. The full league table is presented in Table 7.

P-score rankings placed metformin as the least likely to cause nausea (P-score = 0.712), followed by GLP-1RA + Metformin (P-score = 0.517) and GLP-1RA monotherapy (P-score = 0.271) (Table 3).

**Consistency assessment.** The between-design inconsistency test was not statistically significant (*p* = 0.246); however, within-design heterogeneity was significant (Q_within = 4.52, *p* = 0.034), indicating residual incoherence within the sparse network. Node-splitting tests were non-significant (all *p* ≥ 0.12). ## Heterogeneity Summary

Between-study heterogeneity varied markedly across outcomes (Table 8). # Discussion

# Discussion

## Principal Findings

This network meta-analysis synthesised evidence from 17 randomised controlled trials examining the comparative effectiveness of glucagon-like peptide-1 receptor agonists (GLP-1 RAs), metformin, GLP-1 RA plus metformin combination therapy, and placebo across six clinically relevant outcomes in women with polycystic ovary syndrome (PCOS). The principal finding was that GLP-1 RA plus metformin combination therapy was consistently ranked highest for anthropometric and metabolic outcomes across all networks in which it appeared, achieving P-scores of 0.85 for weight change, 0.85 for BMI change, 0.89 for waist circumference, and 0.90 for HOMA-IR. However, the clinical interpretation of these rankings is substantially constrained by the precision of the underlying estimates. Across the majority of outcomes, confidence intervals for pairwise comparisons versus metformin were wide and crossed the null, indicating that definitive superiority of GLP-1 RA-containing regimens over metformin monotherapy cannot be established from the available evidence. The sole statistically significant pairwise finding was the superiority of GLP-1 RA plus metformin over metformin alone for waist circumference reduction (SMD 0.64, 95% CI 0.06 to 1.22, p = 0.032), a finding of potential clinical relevance given the established role of central adiposity in the pathophysiology of PCOS-related metabolic and reproductive dysfunction [CITATION NEEDED: central adiposity PCOS metabolic reproductive outcomes]. For gastrointestinal adverse events, no statistically significant differences were detected between any treatment pair, though the evidence base was sparse and the confidence intervals were too wide to exclude clinically meaningful differences in tolerability.

## Interpretation in the Context of Existing Literature

The findings of the present analysis are broadly consistent with, yet extend, the conclusions of prior systematic reviews and meta-analyses examining GLP-1 RAs in PCOS. Earlier pairwise meta-analyses have reported that GLP-1 RA monotherapy and combination regimens produce meaningful reductions in body weight and BMI relative to placebo or lifestyle intervention alone [CITATION NEEDED: GLP-1RA PCOS weight BMI meta-analysis], and that these agents may confer additional metabolic benefits beyond those achievable with metformin in insulin-resistant populations [17]. The present network analysis corroborates the directional consistency of these findings—GLP-1 RA-containing strategies ranked above metformin monotherapy for all anthropometric outcomes—but crucially demonstrates that the magnitude of this advantage, when estimated within a formal network framework incorporating both direct and indirect evidence, is imprecise and not statistically robust for weight and BMI endpoints. This discrepancy between directional consistency and statistical imprecision is likely attributable to the extreme between-study heterogeneity observed in the weight and BMI networks (I² = 94.1% and 94.3%, respectively), which substantially inflated standard errors and widened confidence intervals.

The biological plausibility of GLP-1 RA superiority for anthropometric outcomes in PCOS is well established. GLP-1 RAs exert their weight-reducing effects through multiple complementary mechanisms, including appetite suppression via central hypothalamic pathways, delayed gastric emptying, and modulation of energy homeostasis [CITATION NEEDED: GLP-1RA mechanism weight loss appetite suppression]. In women with PCOS, who frequently exhibit hyperinsulinaemia, insulin resistance, and dysregulated appetite signalling, these pharmacological properties may be particularly advantageous [CITATION NEEDED: PCOS hyperinsulinaemia appetite dysregulation pathophysiology]. Furthermore, the combination of GLP-1 RA with metformin may produce synergistic effects on insulin sensitivity through complementary mechanisms—GLP-1 RAs enhancing incretin-mediated insulin secretion and metformin reducing hepatic glucose output and improving peripheral insulin sensitivity—which may partly explain the consistent top-ranking of the combination regimen across metabolic outcomes [CITATION NEEDED: GLP-1RA metformin synergistic insulin sensitivity mechanism].

The finding that HOMA-IR showed no statistically significant differences between treatment groups, despite GLP-1 RA plus metformin achieving the highest P-score (0.90), is noteworthy. The HOMA-IR network was the smallest in the analysis, comprising only three studies, and the absence of detectable heterogeneity (I² = 0%, τ² ≈ 0) in this context likely reflects insufficient statistical power to detect between-study variation rather than genuine homogeneity of treatment effects [CITATION NEEDED: heterogeneity detection power small meta-analysis]. The point estimates for GLP-1 RA monotherapy versus metformin (SMD −0.31, 95% CI −1.05 to 0.42) and for GLP-1 RA plus metformin versus metformin (SMD 0.23, 95% CI −0.11 to 0.57) were directionally inconsistent with one another, suggesting that the network estimate for HOMA-IR may be particularly susceptible to the influence of individual studies. This interpretation is supported by the leave-one-out analyses, which demonstrated that exclusion of any single study left the heterogeneity estimate unchanged at zero, indicating that no single study was disproportionately influential but also that the network lacked the power to detect meaningful sensitivity.

The waist circumference finding warrants particular attention from a clinical standpoint. Central adiposity, as indexed by waist circumference, is an independent predictor of cardiometabolic risk and is mechanistically linked to androgen excess and ovulatory dysfunction in PCOS through its effects on insulin resistance and adipokine dysregulation [CITATION NEEDED: waist circumference cardiometabolic risk PCOS androgen]. The statistically significant advantage of GLP-1 RA plus metformin over metformin alone (SMD 0.64, 95% CI 0.06 to 1.22) in this domain, observed in a network with only moderate heterogeneity (I² = 45.3%) and no detected global inconsistency, represents the most internally consistent and clinically actionable finding of this analysis. Notably, GLP-1 RA monotherapy also demonstrated a numerically meaningful but statistically non-significant advantage over metformin for waist circumference (SMD 0.44, 95% CI −0.18 to 1.07), suggesting that the GLP-1 RA component may be the primary driver of central fat reduction, with metformin co-administration potentially augmenting this effect. These findings are consistent with evidence from non-PCOS populations demonstrating preferential visceral fat reduction with GLP-1 RA therapy [CITATION NEEDED: GLP-1RA visceral fat reduction non-PCOS populations].

With respect to gastrointestinal tolerability, the absence of statistically significant differences in nausea and diarrhoea rates between GLP-1 RA-containing regimens and metformin monotherapy should be interpreted with considerable caution. The nausea network comprised only three studies with five contrasts, and the diarrhoea network only two studies with four contrasts. Both networks exhibited moderate-to-substantial heterogeneity (I² = 65.9% and 77.4%, respectively), and the confidence intervals for all pairwise comparisons were extremely wide, spanning several orders of magnitude on the relative risk scale. For example, the relative risk of nausea with GLP-1 RA monotherapy versus metformin was 0.51 (95% CI 0.08 to 3.27), a range that is entirely uninformative for clinical decision-making. These results should not be interpreted as evidence that GLP-1 RAs are equivalent to metformin in terms of gastrointestinal tolerability. In the broader pharmacological literature, GLP-1 RAs are well recognised to carry a higher burden of nausea and vomiting, particularly during dose titration, relative to placebo, and this profile has been consistently demonstrated in large-scale trials in non-PCOS populations [CITATION NEEDED: GLP-1RA gastrointestinal adverse events nausea vomiting clinical trials]. The failure to detect this signal in the present analysis is most plausibly attributable to inadequate statistical power rather than a genuine absence of effect.

## Heterogeneity and Inconsistency

The extreme heterogeneity observed in the weight and BMI networks (I² > 94%, τ² > 3.4 for both outcomes) represents a fundamental challenge to the interpretability of the pooled estimates and warrants careful consideration of its potential sources. Several factors are likely to have contributed to this variability. First, the included studies employed a range of GLP-1 RA agents—including liraglutide, semaglutide, exenatide, and dulaglutide—which differ substantially in their pharmacokinetic profiles, receptor binding affinities, and dosing regimens, and which may therefore produce heterogeneous weight-loss effects even within the same population [CITATION NEEDED: GLP-1RA pharmacokinetic differences liraglutide semaglutide exenatide dulaglutide]. Second, the enrolled populations varied considerably in baseline BMI, degree of insulin resistance, and PCOS phenotype, all of which are known to moderate the anthropometric response to both GLP-1 RA and metformin therapy [CITATION NEEDED: PCOS phenotype heterogeneity treatment response BMI insulin resistance]. Third, treatment durations ranged from short-term interventions of 12 weeks or fewer to longer-term trials exceeding 24 weeks, and the magnitude of weight loss with GLP-1 RAs is known to be time-dependent, with greater effects emerging over longer treatment periods [CITATION NEEDED: GLP-1RA weight loss time-dependent treatment duration]. Fourth, the use of background lifestyle interventions, which varied in intensity and standardisation across studies, may have differentially amplified or attenuated pharmacological effects on body weight.

The design-decomposition inconsistency tests revealed statistically significant between-design incoherence for both weight change (Q_between = 125.29, p < 0.001) and BMI change (Q_between = 111.51, p < 0.001), indicating that direct and indirect evidence for these outcomes were not fully compatible under the consistency assumption of network meta-analysis [CITATION NEEDED: network meta-analysis consistency assumption design decomposition]. This finding is of considerable methodological importance because it implies that the transitivity assumption—the requirement that the distribution of effect modifiers be exchangeable across treatment comparisons—may have been violated for these outcomes [CITATION NEEDED: transitivity assumption network meta-analysis NMA]. In practical terms, this means that indirect comparisons derived from the network may not accurately represent what would be observed in a direct head-to-head trial, and that the pooled network estimates for weight and BMI should be treated as hypothesis-generating rather than confirmatory. In contrast, node-splitting analyses yielded non-significant p-values for all testable comparisons across all outcomes, suggesting that when individual comparison-level inconsistency was examined, no single comparison was identified as a dominant source of incoherence. This apparent discrepancy between global and local inconsistency tests is not uncommon in small networks and likely reflects the limited power of node-splitting procedures when the number of studies per comparison is small [CITATION NEEDED: node-splitting inconsistency test power network meta-analysis].

For waist circumference and HOMA-IR, the consistency assessment was more reassuring. The waist circumference network showed no statistically significant global inconsistency (Q_total = 3.46, p = 0.177), and all node-splitting comparisons were non-significant, although the network comprised only four studies and the power to detect inconsistency was correspondingly limited. The HOMA-IR network showed no inconsistency signal whatsoever, though again the three-study network was too small to provide meaningful assurance of consistency.

## Transitivity and the Network Meta-Analysis Assumptions

A critical appraisal of the transitivity assumption is essential for interpreting the results of this network meta-analysis. Transitivity requires that patients enrolled in trials comparing GLP-1 RA with placebo, for example, are sufficiently similar in their distribution of effect modifiers to patients enrolled in trials comparing GLP-1 RA with metformin, such that indirect comparisons via the common comparator are valid [CITATION NEEDED: transitivity assumption NMA clinical heterogeneity]. In the present analysis, several potential violations of this assumption were identified. The diagnostic criteria used to define PCOS varied across studies, with some employing the Rotterdam 2003 criteria, others the NIH 1990 criteria, and others the Androgen Excess Society criteria, and these criteria define partially non-overlapping phenotypic groups with different metabolic and reproductive profiles [CITATION NEEDED: PCOS diagnostic criteria Rotterdam NIH AES comparison]. Additionally, the proportion of participants with obesity, the baseline degree of insulin resistance, and the use of concomitant assisted reproductive technology varied across the comparison networks, all of which may act as effect modifiers that differentially influence treatment response across the nodes of the network.

The planned subgroup analyses by GLP-1 RA type, BMI category, treatment duration, and background assisted reproductive technology status were intended to explore potential sources of heterogeneity and effect modification. However, the small number of studies per outcome and the sparse network structure precluded robust subgroup analyses in the present dataset, and these analyses are therefore not reported in the primary results. Future individual patient data meta-analyses would be better positioned to formally test these interactions and to provide more reliable estimates of treatment effects in clinically defined subgroups [CITATION NEEDED: individual patient data meta-analysis subgroup analysis advantages].

## Certainty of Evidence

The certainty of evidence, assessed using the CINeMA framework adapted from GRADE principles for network meta-analysis [CITATION NEEDED: CINeMA framework GRADE network meta-analysis certainty evidence], was rated as very low for weight change, BMI change, nausea, and diarrhoea, and as low for waist circumference and HOMA-IR. For weight and BMI outcomes, certainty was downgraded for very serious inconsistency and heterogeneity, and for serious imprecision, reflecting the wide confidence intervals and the demonstrated incoherence between direct and indirect evidence. For waist circumference, certainty was downgraded for imprecision and the limited number of contributing studies, despite the more favourable heterogeneity profile. For HOMA-IR, downgrading for serious imprecision and sparse evidence was applied despite the absence of detected heterogeneity. For gastrointestinal outcomes, very serious imprecision, sparse events, and heterogeneity concerns collectively justified a very low certainty rating. These ratings indicate that the true effects are likely to differ substantially from the estimated effects, and that the evidence is currently insufficient to support definitive clinical recommendations regarding the superiority of GLP-1 RA-containing regimens over metformin for any of the outcomes examined, with the possible exception of waist circumference where the certainty, while low, is somewhat higher.

## Strengths

This network meta-analysis has several methodological strengths. The use of a pre-specified PICO framework with explicit diagnostic criteria for PCOS, including the requirement for Rotterdam 2003, NIH 1990, or AE-PCOS criteria, ensured that the enrolled population was clinically homogeneous with respect to disease definition. The restriction to randomised controlled trials minimised the risk of confounding inherent in observational evidence. The network meta-analysis framework, implemented using the frequentist *netmeta* package with restricted maximum likelihood estimation of heterogeneity, enabled simultaneous comparison of all four treatment nodes and the generation of treatment rankings via P-scores, which provide a more nuanced summary of comparative effectiveness than pairwise analyses alone [CITATION NEEDED: P-score treatment ranking network meta-analysis frequentist]. The comprehensive assessment of consistency using both design-decomposition and node-splitting approaches, combined with leave-one-out sensitivity analyses, provided a thorough evaluation of the robustness of the network assumptions. The absence of language or date restrictions in the search strategy reduced the risk of language bias and ensured that the evidence base was as complete as possible [CITATION NEEDED: language restriction bias systematic review].

## Limitations

Several limitations must be acknowledged. First, and most fundamentally, the extreme heterogeneity in the weight and BMI networks renders the pooled estimates for these outcomes of limited clinical utility. The τ² values of 3.42 and 3.45 for weight and BMI, respectively, imply that prediction intervals—had they been formally computed—would have been extremely wide and would likely have encompassed no effect, meaning that the average network estimate provides little guidance about what effect would be expected in a new clinical setting or patient population [CITATION NEEDED: prediction interval heterogeneity meta-analysis clinical interpretation]. Second, the number of studies contributing to each outcome network was small, ranging from two to ten studies, which limited the power of all statistical tests, inflated the uncertainty of heterogeneity estimates, and precluded reliable assessment of publication bias through funnel plot asymmetry [CITATION NEEDED: funnel plot small study effects publication bias meta-analysis]. Although comparison-adjusted funnel plots were generated, formal asymmetry testing with fewer than ten studies per comparison is unreliable and was therefore not interpreted [CITATION NEEDED: Egger test funnel plot minimum studies]. Third, the use of standardised mean differences as the effect measure for continuous outcomes, necessitated by the heterogeneity of measurement instruments and reporting formats across studies, introduces an additional layer of interpretive complexity and may amplify apparent heterogeneity when the variability of the outcome measure differs systematically across treatment groups or study populations [CITATION NEEDED: standardised mean difference limitations heterogeneity meta-analysis]. Fourth, the primary outcomes of ovulation rate, clinical pregnancy rate, live birth rate, and miscarriage rate—which are of central importance to women with PCOS seeking fertility treatment—could not be synthesised in the network meta-analysis due to insufficient data, representing a critical evidence gap that substantially limits the clinical applicability of the present findings for reproductive endpoints. Fifth, the included studies varied in their use of background assisted reproductive technology, lifestyle co-interventions, and concomitant medications, which may have differentially influenced outcomes across the comparison networks and contributed to the observed inconsistency. Sixth, the leave-one-out analyses, while informative, were limited in scope, and the sensitivity of results to the exclusion of high risk-of-bias studies could not be fully evaluated from the available data. Finally, the absence of individual patient data precluded formal exploration of treatment effect heterogeneity by clinically relevant subgroups, including GLP-1 RA type, baseline BMI, treatment duration, and PCOS phenotype, all of which are plausible moderators of treatment response.

## Implications for Clinical Practice

In the context of the available evidence, the following tentative clinical implications may be drawn, with the explicit caveat that all are based on low to very low certainty evidence. GLP-1 RA plus metformin combination therapy may offer a modest advantage over metformin monotherapy for reducing central adiposity, as reflected by waist circumference, in women with PCOS. This finding is clinically relevant because central adiposity is a key driver of the metabolic and reproductive complications of PCOS, and its reduction may contribute to improvements in insulin sensitivity, androgen excess, and ovulatory function [CITATION NEEDED: central adiposity reduction PCOS metabolic reproductive improvement]. For weight and BMI outcomes, the directional consistency of treatment rankings in favour of GLP-1 RA-containing regimens is encouraging, but the statistical imprecision and extreme heterogeneity of the network estimates preclude confident clinical recommendations. Clinicians considering GLP-1 RA therapy in women with PCOS should be aware that the evidence base for superiority over metformin remains uncertain, that gastrointestinal tolerability differences between regimens could not be reliably quantified in this analysis, and that the reproductive outcomes most relevant to patients—including ovulation rate, pregnancy rate, and live birth rate—remain insufficiently studied in the context of randomised controlled trials [8]. The choice between GLP-1 RA monotherapy, metformin monotherapy, and combination therapy should therefore be individualised based on patient-specific factors including degree of obesity, insulin resistance, reproductive goals, and tolerability preferences, pending the availability of higher-certainty evidence.

## Implications for Future Research

The findings of this network meta-analysis highlight several priorities for future research. First, adequately powered randomised controlled trials with standardised outcome reporting are urgently needed, particularly for reproductive endpoints including ovulation rate, clinical pregnancy rate, and live birth rate, which could not be synthesised in the present analysis. Second, future trials should employ consistent diagnostic criteria for PCOS, standardised definitions of treatment response, and pre-specified subgroup analyses by GLP-1 RA agent, baseline BMI, and PCOS phenotype to facilitate more reliable evidence synthesis. Third, head-to-head trials directly comparing individual GLP-1 RA agents—particularly the newer, more potent agents such as semaglutide and tirzepatide—with metformin in PCOS populations are needed to establish whether agent-specific differences in efficacy and tolerability exist [CITATION NEEDED: semaglutide tirzepatide PCOS emerging evidence]. Fourth, longer-term trials examining the durability of anthropometric and metabolic benefits, as well as the impact of GLP-1 RA therapy on reproductive outcomes in women undergoing assisted reproduction, would substantially advance the evidence base. Fifth, individual patient data meta-analyses, which would enable formal testing of treatment effect heterogeneity by clinically relevant subgroups and the computation of reliable prediction intervals, should be prioritised as the evidence base expands [CITATION NEEDED: individual patient data meta-analysis network meta-analysis advantages]. Finally, the development and validation of composite outcome measures that integrate anthropometric, metabolic, and reproductive endpoints would facilitate more clinically meaningful evidence synthesis in future systematic reviews of PCOS pharmacotherapy.

## Conclusion

This network meta-analysis provides a comprehensive synthesis of the comparative effectiveness and tolerability of GLP-1 receptor agonists, metformin, and their combination in women with PCOS. GLP-1 RA plus metformin was consistently ranked highest for anthropometric and metabolic outcomes, and demonstrated a statistically significant advantage over metformin monotherapy for waist circumference reduction. However, for weight change, BMI change, HOMA-IR, and gastrointestinal adverse events, the evidence was characterised by extreme heterogeneity, important inconsistency, and serious imprecision, yielding low to very low certainty estimates that preclude definitive clinical conclusions. The reproductive outcomes of greatest relevance to women with PCOS could not be synthesised due to insufficient data, representing a critical gap in the current evidence base. Future well-designed, adequately powered randomised controlled trials with standardised reproductive and metabolic outcome reporting are essential to establish the role of GLP-1 receptor agonists in the management of PCOS.

# Conclusion

# Conclusions

## Summary of Principal Findings

This network meta-analysis of randomized controlled trials evaluating glucagon-like peptide-1 receptor agonists (GLP-1 RAs) in women with polycystic ovary syndrome (PCOS) synthesized evidence across six outcomes from a network comprising up to four treatment nodes: GLP-1 RA monotherapy, GLP-1 RA plus metformin combination therapy, metformin monotherapy, and placebo. Across anthropometric and metabolic outcomes, GLP-1 RA plus metformin consistently achieved the highest P-score rankings, followed by GLP-1 RA monotherapy, metformin, and placebo. However, the clinical interpretation of these rankings is substantially constrained by pervasive imprecision, extreme between-study heterogeneity, and evidence of network inconsistency in the largest outcome networks.

The single outcome for which a statistically significant network estimate was demonstrated was waist circumference, where GLP-1 RA plus metformin was superior to metformin monotherapy (SMD 0.64, 95% CI 0.06 to 1.22; p = 0.032; P-score = 0.89), suggesting a potentially clinically meaningful reduction in central adiposity with combination therapy. For weight change (kg) and BMI change (kg/m²), despite the favorable ranking of GLP-1 RA-containing regimens, network estimates versus metformin were imprecise and did not achieve statistical significance (weight: SMD 0.73, 95% CI −0.81 to 2.28 for GLP-1 RA plus metformin; BMI: SMD 0.77, 95% CI −0.94 to 2.48), and both networks were characterized by very high heterogeneity (I² = 94.1% and 94.3%, respectively; τ² ≈ 3.42–3.45) and statistically significant design-by-treatment inconsistency (Q_between p < 0.001 for both). For insulin resistance as measured by HOMA-IR, no significant differences were observed versus metformin for either GLP-1 RA monotherapy (SMD −0.31, 95% CI −1.05 to 0.42) or combination therapy (SMD 0.23, 95% CI −0.11 to 0.57), though this network was reassuringly homogeneous (I² = 0%; τ² ≈ 0). Gastrointestinal adverse events, including nausea and diarrhea, did not differ significantly across treatment nodes, with risk ratios characterized by very wide confidence intervals that precluded any definitive conclusions regarding the comparative tolerability of GLP-1 RA-containing regimens relative to metformin.

## Implications for Clinical Practice

The present findings do not provide sufficient evidence to recommend GLP-1 RA monotherapy or GLP-1 RA plus metformin combination therapy as superior to metformin monotherapy for the majority of outcomes examined in women with PCOS. The one exception is waist circumference, where the combination regimen demonstrated a statistically significant, albeit modest, advantage. Given that central adiposity is a recognized driver of metabolic and reproductive dysfunction in PCOS [CITATION NEEDED: central adiposity metabolic dysfunction PCOS], this finding may carry clinical relevance, particularly for women with obesity-predominant phenotypes. Nevertheless, the small number of contributing studies (n = 4), moderate residual heterogeneity (I² = 45.3%), and low certainty of evidence (GRADE: low) necessitate cautious interpretation.

Metformin remains the most extensively studied pharmacological agent for metabolic management in PCOS [CITATION NEEDED: metformin PCOS metabolic outcomes systematic review], and the current evidence does not establish a consistent or statistically robust advantage of GLP-1 RA-based strategies over this established comparator. Clinicians considering GLP-1 RA therapy in this population should weigh the uncertain magnitude of metabolic benefit against the tolerability profile of these agents, noting that the available evidence was insufficient to characterize gastrointestinal adverse event rates with precision. The emerging evidence base for newer GLP-1 RA agents, particularly semaglutide and dulaglutide, in PCOS remains limited [CITATION NEEDED: semaglutide dulaglutide PCOS RCT], and extrapolation from broader obesity or type 2 diabetes trial populations should be undertaken with caution given the distinct pathophysiology of PCOS [CITATION NEEDED: PCOS pathophysiology insulin resistance hyperandrogenism].

## Implications for Future Research

The findings of this review identify several critical gaps that future research must address. First, the extreme heterogeneity observed for weight and BMI outcomes (I² > 94%) indicates that pooled estimates from the current evidence base are unlikely to be reliable guides to the true treatment effect in any specific clinical context. Future trials should adopt standardized outcome measurement protocols, reporting time points, and patient selection criteria to reduce between-study variability [CITATION NEEDED: PCOS outcome standardization core outcome set]. Second, the detection of statistically significant network inconsistency for weight and BMI outcomes highlights the need for adequately powered, multi-arm randomized trials that can provide direct head-to-head comparisons across all relevant treatment nodes, thereby reducing reliance on indirect evidence chains that may violate the transitivity assumption [CITATION NEEDED: transitivity assumption network meta-analysis methods].

Third, the evidence base for reproductive outcomes—including ovulation rate, clinical pregnancy rate, and live birth rate—was insufficient to support quantitative synthesis in this review, representing a fundamental gap given that restoration of ovulatory function is a primary therapeutic goal in PCOS [CITATION NEEDED: PCOS ovulation induction treatment goals]. Future trials should pre-specify and rigorously measure confirmed ovulation (via mid-luteal serum progesterone or ultrasound) as a primary endpoint, and should be powered to detect clinically meaningful differences in live birth rates. Fourth, subgroup analyses by GLP-1 RA agent type (liraglutide, semaglutide, exenatide, dulaglutide), BMI category, treatment duration, and use of assisted reproductive technology were planned but could not be conducted robustly due to insufficient data; future individual participant data meta-analyses would be particularly valuable for exploring effect modification by these clinically important variables [CITATION NEEDED: individual participant data meta-analysis advantages subgroup].

Fifth, the certainty of evidence across all outcomes was rated as low to very low according to GRADE criteria, driven principally by serious to very serious concerns regarding inconsistency, imprecision, and heterogeneity. Upgrading the certainty of evidence will require larger, methodologically rigorous trials with low risk of bias, pre-registered protocols, and adherence to established reporting standards such as CONSORT [CITATION NEEDED: CONSORT reporting RCT guidelines]. Finally, the long-term safety and tolerability of GLP-1 RA therapy in reproductive-age women with PCOS, including effects on menstrual cycle regularity, hormonal profiles, and pregnancy outcomes, remain inadequately characterized and should be prioritized in future research agendas [CITATION NEEDED: GLP-1RA long term safety reproductive age women].

## Conclusions

In conclusion, this network meta-analysis provides low to very low certainty evidence that GLP-1 RA plus metformin combination therapy may reduce waist circumference to a greater extent than metformin monotherapy in women with PCOS, representing the most robust signal identified across the outcomes examined. For weight, BMI, insulin resistance, and gastrointestinal tolerability, no statistically significant or clinically reliable advantages of GLP-1 RA-containing regimens over metformin were demonstrated, and estimates were characterized by extreme heterogeneity and, in the case of anthropometric outcomes, significant network inconsistency. The current evidence base is insufficient to support a change in clinical practice guidelines favoring GLP-1 RAs over metformin as first-line pharmacological therapy for metabolic outcomes in PCOS. Adequately powered, methodologically rigorous randomized controlled trials with standardized outcome measurement and pre-specified reproductive endpoints are urgently needed to resolve the substantial uncertainty that persists in this clinically important area.
