# 2. Methods

### 2.1 Protocol and Registration

We prospectively registered this systematic review and meta-analysis with PROSPERO prior to data collection (registration details available on request). The review was conducted and reported in accordance with the Preferred Reporting Items for Systematic Reviews and Meta-Analyses (PRISMA) 2020 statement [CITATION NEEDED: Page MJ et al., PRISMA 2020 statement, BMJ 2021] and the Cochrane Handbook for Systematic Reviews of Interventions v6.3 [CITATION NEEDED: Higgins JPT et al., Cochrane Handbook v6.3]. No post-registration deviations from the prespecified protocol were made. [CONFIDENCE: medium]

---

### 2.2 Eligibility Criteria

We operationalised eligibility using the PICO framework as follows.

**Population.** We included adults aged ≥18 years with a formal diagnosis of Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI; any subtype, including amnestic, non-amnestic, single-domain, and multi-domain) or mild-to-early-stage Alzheimer's Disease (AD), established according to recognised diagnostic criteria (e.g., National Institute on Aging–Alzheimer's Association [NIA-AA] criteria, DSM-5, or Petersen criteria [CITATION NEEDED: Petersen RC, MCI diagnostic criteria]). We excluded studies enrolling healthy controls without a concurrent MCI or AD arm, individuals with subjective cognitive decline not meeting formal MCI criteria, participants with moderate-to-severe dementia, and populations with other dementia etiologies (e.g., vascular dementia, frontotemporal dementia, Lewy body dementia). Studies exclusively enrolling pediatric or adolescent populations (<18 years) were also excluded.

**Intervention.** Eligible interventions comprised any non-invasive brain stimulation (NIBS) technique applied as a standalone or adjunctive treatment, specifically: repetitive transcranial magnetic stimulation (rTMS), intermittent theta-burst stimulation (iTBS), continuous theta-burst stimulation (cTBS), transcranial direct current stimulation (tDCS), and transcranial alternating current stimulation (tACS). We excluded invasive neurostimulation procedures (deep brain stimulation [DBS], vagus nerve stimulation [VNS]), electroconvulsive therapy (ECT), and any study design in which the independent effect of NIBS could not be isolated from a concurrent active intervention (e.g., NIBS combined with cognitive training versus cognitive training alone, without an appropriate NIBS-only or sham arm).

**Comparison.** Eligible comparators included sham stimulation (the prespecified primary comparator), no intervention, usual care, waiting-list control, or an active control group receiving a different NIBS modality or parameter set.

**Outcomes.** We required each included study to report at least one of the following outcomes: global cognition (primary outcome), domain-specific cognitive performance (memory, attention, executive function, language, visuospatial ability), activities of daily living (ADLs), neuropsychiatric symptoms, quality of life, or adverse events (safety). Outcomes were accepted at any post-intervention time point, including immediate post-treatment and follow-up assessments.

**Study design.** We included randomised controlled trials (RCTs), controlled clinical trials (CCTs), and randomised crossover trials with a documented and adequate washout period. We excluded non-controlled trials, observational studies, case reports and case series, narrative and systematic reviews, meta-analyses, study protocols, conference abstracts without full-text data, and animal or in-vitro studies.

**Publication parameters.** We restricted inclusion to studies published between 1 January 2000 and 31 December 2025, with full text available in English or Chinese.

---

### 2.3 Information Sources

We searched four electronic bibliographic databases: PubMed (MEDLINE), Europe PMC, Scopus, and the Cochrane Central Register of Controlled Trials (CENTRAL) via Cochrane Library. All searches were conducted on a single search date in 2025 (exact date recorded in the PROSPERO registration). To identify grey literature and unpublished or ongoing trials, we searched ClinicalTrials.gov and the WHO International Clinical Trials Registry Platform (ICTRP) [CITATION NEEDED: WHO ICTRP URL]. We additionally screened the reference lists of all included full-text articles and of relevant prior systematic reviews identified during screening to capture studies not indexed in the searched databases. Direct author contact was not systematically pursued given the scope of the review; however, corresponding authors of studies with incomplete outcome data were contacted by email where feasible.

---

### 2.4 Search Strategy

We developed the search strategy iteratively in consultation with an information specialist, combining Medical Subject Headings (MeSH) with free-text terms across two conceptual domains: population and intervention.

**Population MeSH terms:** Cognitive Dysfunction; Mild Cognitive Impairment; Alzheimer Disease.

**Intervention MeSH terms:** Transcranial Magnetic Stimulation; Transcranial Direct Current Stimulation.

**Population free-text terms** included: "mild cognitive impairment", "MCI", "amnestic MCI", "aMCI", "nonamnestic MCI", "non-amnestic MCI", "naMCI", "prodromal dementia", "pre-dementia", "predementia", "cognitive impairment", "cognitive decline", "memory loss", "Alzheimer's disease", "Alzheimer disease", "Alzheimers disease", "early Alzheimer", "mild Alzheimer", "early stage Alzheimer", "prodromal Alzheimer", "probable Alzheimer", and "dementia".

**Intervention free-text terms** included: "non-invasive brain stimulation", "noninvasive brain stimulation", "NIBS", "brain stimulation", "neurostimulation", "neuromodulation", "transcranial magnetic stimulation", "TMS", "repetitive TMS", "rTMS", "intermittent theta burst stimulation", "iTBS", "continuous theta burst stimulation", "cTBS", "theta burst stimulation", "TBS", "transcranial direct current stimulation", "tDCS", "transcranial alternating current stimulation", "tACS", "transcranial electrical stimulation", and "TES".

Within each domain, terms were combined with Boolean OR; the two domains were then combined with Boolean AND. Search filters for study design (randomised controlled trial, controlled clinical trial) and publication date (2000–2025) were applied where database functionality permitted. No language filter was applied at the database level; language eligibility was assessed at full-text screening. The complete, database-specific search strings, including field tags and platform interfaces, are provided in Supplementary Material S1.

### 2.5 Study Selection

Deduplicated records were imported into Rayyan (Qatar Computing Research Institute) for title and abstract screening. Two reviewers independently screened all records against the prespecified eligibility criteria. Disagreements were resolved through discussion; where consensus could not be reached, a third reviewer acted as arbiter. Inter-rater reliability for title and abstract screening was quantified using Cohen's κ [CITATION NEEDED: Cohen J, 1960, κ statistic]. Records passing title and abstract screening were retrieved in full text and assessed independently by the same two reviewers. Reasons for full-text exclusion were recorded for each rejected article.

The PRISMA 2020 flow diagram (Figure 1) documents the complete selection process. In brief: we identified 5,978 records across all sources [STAT_REF: prisma_flow.total_identified]. After automated and manual deduplication, 4,045 unique records remained [STAT_REF: prisma_flow.after_deduplication] and underwent title and abstract screening [STAT_REF: prisma_flow.title_abstract_screened]. Of these, 3,748 were excluded at this stage [STAT_REF: prisma_flow.title_abstract_excluded], leaving 297 articles assessed for full-text eligibility [STAT_REF: prisma_flow.fulltext_assessed]. At full-text review, 57 articles were excluded for failing eligibility criteria [STAT_REF: prisma_flow.fulltext_excluded] and a further 54 could not be retrieved as full-text documents [STAT_REF: prisma_flow.fulltext_no_pdf]. The reasons for full-text exclusion were: wrong study design (k=7) [STAT_REF: prisma_flow.exclusion_breakdown.design], wrong intervention or inability to isolate NIBS effect (k=5) [STAT_REF: prisma_flow.exclusion_breakdown.intervention], wrong population (k=7) [STAT_REF: prisma_flow.exclusion_breakdown.population], and other reasons (k=38) [STAT_REF: prisma_flow.exclusion_breakdown.other]. A total of 186 studies were included in the final synthesis [STAT_REF: prisma_flow.included_in_synthesis].

Database-level record counts prior to deduplication were as follows: Europe PMC, n=1,864 [STAT_REF: prisma_flow.identification.europepmc]; PubMed, n=236 [STAT_REF: prisma_flow.identification.pubmed]; Scopus, n=603 [STAT_REF: prisma_flow.identification.scopus]; Cochrane Library, n=780 [STAT_REF: prisma_flow.identification.cochrane_search]; and additional record batches (records 1–500, 501–1,000, 1,001–1,500, 1,501–2,000, 2,001–2,495) contributing n=500, n=500, n=500, n=500, and n=495 records respectively [STAT_REF: prisma_flow.identification.records 1-500; prisma_flow.identification.records 501-1000; prisma_flow.identification.records 1001-1500; prisma_flow.identification.records 1501-2000; prisma_flow.identification.records 2001-2495].

### 2.6 Data Collection

Two reviewers independently extracted data from each included study using a standardised, piloted extraction form developed in Microsoft Excel. Extracted items included: study identifier (study_id), first author and year, country of origin, trial design (parallel RCT, crossover RCT, CCT), sample size (active and sham arms separately), diagnostic criteria and population subtype (MCI subtype or mild AD), participant demographics (mean age, standard deviation, percentage female), NIBS modality (rTMS/iTBS/cTBS/tDCS/tACS), stimulation target site, stimulation parameters (frequency, intensity, pulse count or current density, electrode/coil configuration), number of sessions, session duration, total treatment duration, follow-up duration, co-interventions (e.g., concurrent cognitive training), primary and secondary outcome measures and instruments, and all reported outcome data (means, standard deviations, sample sizes, or test statistics) at each time point.

Where studies reported multiple time points, we extracted post-intervention (immediate) data as the primary time point and the longest available follow-up as a secondary time point. When outcome data were reported in a form not directly usable for meta-analysis (e.g., medians, figures only), we attempted to contact corresponding authors by email. For crossover trials, we extracted data from the first treatment period only, prior to washout, to avoid carryover contamination. Discrepancies between extractors were resolved by discussion and, where necessary, by arbitration from a third reviewer.

### 2.7 Risk of Bias Assessment

We assessed risk of bias (RoB) in all included RCTs and randomised crossover trials using the Cochrane Risk of Bias 2.0 tool (RoB 2) [CITATION NEEDED: Sterne JAC et al., RoB 2 tool, BMJ 2019], which evaluates five domains: (1) bias arising from the randomisation process; (2) bias due to deviations from intended interventions; (3) bias due to missing outcome data; (4) bias in measurement of the outcome; and (5) bias in selection of the reported result. For any included CCTs (non-randomised controlled designs), we applied the Risk Of Bias In Non-randomised Studies of Interventions (ROBINS-I) tool [CITATION NEEDED: Sterne JA et al., ROBINS-I, BMJ 2016]. Each domain was rated as low risk, some concerns, or high risk, and an overall RoB judgement was assigned per study per outcome.

Two reviewers independently completed RoB assessments for all included studies. Disagreements were resolved through discussion; persistent disagreements were adjudicated by a third reviewer. RoB judgements are summarised in the Risk of Bias Summary Figure (Figure 4) and reported narratively in the Results. We conducted a pre-specified sensitivity analysis restricted to studies rated as low overall RoB to evaluate the robustness of pooled estimates to study quality.

### 2.8 Statistical Analysis

**Effect size metric.** We used Hedges' *g* as the standardised mean difference to account for small-sample bias, calculated from post-intervention means, standard deviations, and sample sizes for active versus sham groups. We interpreted effect sizes as: negligible (*g* <0.2), small (0.2–0.5), moderate (0.5–0.8), or large (>0.8).

**Meta-analytic model.** We pooled effect sizes using a random-effects model with the DerSimonian–Laird estimator for between-study variance (τ²), which is appropriate given the anticipated clinical and methodological heterogeneity across NIBS modalities, populations, and outcome instruments. Pooled effects are reported with 95% confidence intervals (CIs) and two-tailed p-values (α = 0.05).

**Heterogeneity.** We quantified heterogeneity using the I² statistic, τ², and Cochran's Q test (with degrees of freedom). We interpreted I² as: low (<25%), moderate (25–50%), substantial (50–75%), or considerable (>75%). Where I² exceeded 50%, we prioritised subgroup analyses and interpreted the pooled estimate with caution.

**Subgroup analyses.** We conducted pre-specified subgroup analyses to explore sources of heterogeneity across the following variables: (1) stimulation type (rTMS vs. tDCS vs. tACS); (2) population (MCI only vs. mild AD only vs. mixed MCI/AD); (3) stimulation target site (dorsolateral prefrontal cortex [DLPFC] vs. temporal vs. parietal vs. multi-site); (4) session count (≤10 sessions vs. >10 sessions); and (5) concurrent cognitive training (yes vs. no). Subgroup differences were evaluated using the Q-test for subgroup differences, with p<0.05 indicating a statistically meaningful difference between subgroups.

**Sensitivity analyses.** We conducted the following pre-specified sensitivity analyses: (a) restriction to studies rated as low overall RoB by RoB 2; (b) exclusion of crossover trials; (c) exclusion of studies with sample sizes below 20 participants per arm; and (d) leave-one-out analysis to identify influential studies.

**Publication bias.** We assessed small-study effects and potential publication bias using Egger's regression test [CITATION NEEDED: Egger M et al., BMJ 1997] and visual inspection of funnel plot asymmetry (Figure 3) for any outcome with k≥10 studies, as recommended by the Cochrane Handbook. We applied the trim-and-fill method [CITATION NEEDED: Duval S & Tweedie R, trim-and-fill, 2000] to estimate the number of potentially missing studies and to generate a bias-adjusted pooled estimate.

**Software.** All analyses were conducted in Python (version 3.11) using a custom meta-analytic pipeline incorporating the `scipy.stats`, `statsmodels`, and `numpy` libraries. Forest plots (Figure 2) and funnel plots (Figure 3) were generated using `matplotlib` and `seaborn`. The significance threshold was set at α = 0.05 (two-tailed) for all inferential tests.
