# Introduction

---

### Disease Burden and Clinical Context

Dementia represents one of the most pressing public health challenges of the twenty-first century. Alzheimer's disease (AD) accounts for approximately 60–70% of all dementia cases worldwide [CITATION NEEDED: WHO World Alzheimer Report prevalence estimates], affecting an estimated 55 million people globally, a figure projected to exceed 139 million by 2050 as populations age [CITATION NEEDED: Alzheimer's Disease International Global Prevalence Report]. The economic cost of dementia care exceeds USD 1.3 trillion annually, with the majority borne by informal caregivers and health systems in low- and middle-income countries [CITATION NEEDED: global cost-of-dementia estimates]. Beyond its economic toll, AD imposes profound burdens on patients, families, and caregivers, eroding autonomy, identity, and quality of life across years of progressive decline.

Mild cognitive impairment (MCI) occupies a clinically critical position on the AD continuum. Defined by objective cognitive decline exceeding age-expected norms without functional impairment sufficient to meet dementia criteria [CITATION NEEDED: Petersen criteria and NIA-AA MCI diagnostic framework], MCI affects an estimated 15–20% of adults aged 65 years and older [CITATION NEEDED: MCI prevalence meta-analysis]. Amnestic MCI (aMCI), characterised by predominant episodic memory impairment, carries an annual conversion rate to AD dementia of approximately 10–15% [CITATION NEEDED: MCI-to-AD conversion rate longitudinal studies], making it a priority target for disease-modifying and cognitive-preserving interventions. Early-stage AD, in turn, represents the earliest symptomatic phase of established disease, during which neuronal networks retain sufficient plasticity to respond to therapeutic modulation [CITATION NEEDED: synaptic plasticity in early AD neuropathology].

Current pharmacological options for MCI and early AD remain limited in scope and effect. Cholinesterase inhibitors (ChEIs) and memantine, the mainstays of symptomatic AD management, produce modest and often transient cognitive benefits, do not alter underlying neuropathology, and carry dose-limiting adverse effects [CITATION NEEDED: Cochrane review of ChEIs in AD and MCI]. The recent approval of anti-amyloid monoclonal antibodies (lecanemab, donanemab) represents a mechanistic advance, yet these agents are associated with amyloid-related imaging abnormalities (ARIA), require intravenous administration, and remain inaccessible to most patients globally due to cost and infrastructure constraints [CITATION NEEDED: FDA approval and safety data for lecanemab/donanemab]. No pharmacological agent has demonstrated robust, durable improvement in global cognition or functional independence in MCI. This therapeutic gap has intensified interest in non-pharmacological approaches capable of directly modulating the neural circuits implicated in memory and executive function.

### The Intervention: Rationale and Mechanisms

Non-invasive brain stimulation (NIBS) encompasses a family of techniques that modulate cortical and subcortical excitability through externally applied electromagnetic or electrical fields, without surgical intervention. The three principal modalities studied in cognitive disorders are repetitive transcranial magnetic stimulation (rTMS), transcranial direct current stimulation (tDCS), and transcranial alternating current stimulation (tACS), each operating through distinct but partially overlapping mechanisms.

rTMS delivers pulsed magnetic fields through a coil positioned over the scalp, inducing transient electrical currents in underlying cortical tissue. High-frequency rTMS (≥5 Hz) generally increases cortical excitability, while low-frequency protocols (≤1 Hz) are inhibitory [CITATION NEEDED: Hallett rTMS mechanisms review]. Theta-burst stimulation (TBS), a patterned variant of rTMS, delivers bursts at theta frequency (5 Hz) and achieves comparable neuroplastic effects in substantially shorter treatment durations; intermittent TBS (iTBS) is excitatory and continuous TBS (cTBS) inhibitory [CITATION NEEDED: Huang et al. TBS original characterisation]. Deep rTMS using H-coils extends the stimulation field to deeper cortical and limbic structures, potentially engaging hippocampal circuits more directly than conventional figure-of-eight coils [CITATION NEEDED: deep rTMS H-coil penetration depth studies]. tDCS applies weak, constant direct current (typically 1–2 mA) via scalp electrodes, with anodal stimulation increasing and cathodal stimulation decreasing neuronal firing thresholds through subthreshold membrane polarisation [CITATION NEEDED: Nitsche and Paulus tDCS mechanism original papers]. High-definition tDCS (HD-tDCS) uses smaller, focally arranged electrodes to improve spatial precision. tACS entrains endogenous neural oscillations at the applied frequency, with gamma-frequency tACS (40 Hz) attracting particular interest for its potential to restore pathologically reduced gamma oscillations observed in AD cortex [CITATION NEEDED: gamma oscillation deficits in AD and 40 Hz tACS rationale].

The theoretical rationale for NIBS in MCI and early AD is grounded in the neuroscience of synaptic plasticity and network dysfunction. AD is characterised by progressive disruption of cholinergic and glutamatergic neurotransmission, reduced long-term potentiation (LTP), and degradation of large-scale functional networks including the default mode network and the hippocampal-entorhinal memory circuit [CITATION NEEDED: AD network dysfunction neuroimaging reviews]. NIBS protocols targeting the dorsolateral prefrontal cortex (DLPFC), left or bilateral parietal cortex, and precuneus are hypothesised to upregulate synaptic efficacy, enhance cholinergic tone, and restore functional connectivity within memory-relevant networks [CITATION NEEDED: NIBS target site rationale in cognitive disorders]. Neuronavigation-guided stimulation, increasingly employed in recent trials, allows individualised targeting based on structural or functional MRI, potentially improving both precision and therapeutic yield [CITATION NEEDED: neuronavigated TMS in cognitive research]. The combination of NIBS with concurrent cognitive training has also emerged as a promising strategy, leveraging the Hebbian principle that stimulation-induced plasticity is amplified when paired with task-relevant neural activity [CITATION NEEDED: NIBS combined with cognitive training synergy evidence].

### Existing Evidence and Its Limitations

The past decade has witnessed a rapid accumulation of randomised controlled trials (RCTs) examining NIBS for cognitive enhancement in MCI and AD, prompting several systematic reviews and meta-analyses. Early meta-analyses, published between 2017 and 2020, reported generally favourable but heterogeneous effects of rTMS and tDCS on global cognition and memory, with pooled effect sizes in the small-to-moderate range [CITATION NEEDED: early meta-analyses of rTMS/tDCS in AD and MCI, e.g., Hsu et al., Tatti et al., Nguyen et al.]. More recent syntheses have extended these findings to specific cognitive domains including working memory, attention, and language, and have begun to examine neuropsychiatric outcomes such as apathy and depression [CITATION NEEDED: recent NIBS meta-analyses 2021–2024].

Despite this growing body of evidence, prior reviews are limited by several important methodological and scope-related constraints. First, most existing meta-analyses have examined a single NIBS modality in isolation—typically rTMS or tDCS—precluding direct cross-modality comparisons and a comprehensive understanding of the relative efficacy of different stimulation approaches [CITATION NEEDED: single-modality NIBS reviews]. Second, many reviews have conflated MCI and AD populations, or have included moderate-to-severe dementia alongside early-stage disease, obscuring potentially important stage-specific treatment effects and limiting the clinical applicability of findings to the populations most likely to benefit from neuroplasticity-based interventions. Third, the rapid pace of trial publication means that reviews conducted before 2022 are likely to have missed a substantial proportion of the available evidence, particularly trials of newer modalities such as gamma-frequency tACS, HD-tDCS, and accelerated iTBS protocols. Fourth, methodological quality assessment in prior reviews has varied considerably, with inconsistent application of contemporary risk of bias tools such as the Cochrane RoB 2.0 framework, limiting confidence in pooled estimates. Fifth, secondary outcomes of direct clinical relevance—including functional independence, neuropsychiatric symptom burden, quality of life, and caregiver outcomes—have received limited attention in prior syntheses, despite their centrality to patient and caregiver priorities. Finally, publication bias, a pervasive concern in the NIBS literature given the preponderance of small single-centre trials, has not been systematically evaluated across the full spectrum of NIBS modalities and outcomes in any prior review [CITATION NEEDED: publication bias concerns in NIBS literature].

### Knowledge Gap and Objectives

The convergence of an expanding trial base, the emergence of novel stimulation modalities, and the persistent limitations of prior reviews creates a clear imperative for a comprehensive, methodologically rigorous synthesis. No existing meta-analysis has simultaneously (1) encompassed all major NIBS modalities—rTMS, tDCS, tACS, and their variants—within a single analytical framework; (2) restricted the population to MCI and early-stage AD, the clinical stages at which neuroplasticity-based interventions are most mechanistically plausible; (3) incorporated the full breadth of trials published through 2024; and (4) systematically evaluated both cognitive and non-cognitive outcomes alongside safety and tolerability using contemporary risk of bias methodology.

We therefore conducted a pre-registered systematic review and meta-analysis to address this gap. Our objectives were threefold. First, we aimed to synthesise the available evidence on the efficacy of NIBS—encompassing rTMS (including iTBS and cTBS), tDCS (including HD-tDCS), and tACS—compared with sham stimulation or active control conditions, for improving global cognition and specific cognitive domains in adults with MCI or early-stage AD. Second, we aimed to characterise the safety and tolerability profile of NIBS in this population, and to evaluate secondary outcomes including functional independence, neuropsychiatric symptoms, and quality of life. Third, we aimed to explore potential sources of between-study heterogeneity through pre-specified subgroup analyses examining modality, stimulation target, frequency, session number, and diagnostic category, with the goal of identifying the stimulation parameters and patient characteristics most likely to predict therapeutic benefit. The present review, encompassing 186 studies and 6,007 participants [STAT_REF: data_context.studies_included; data_context.total_participants], represents the most comprehensive synthesis of NIBS for MCI and early AD conducted to date, and is intended to inform both clinical practice and the design of future large-scale trials.
