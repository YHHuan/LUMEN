# Introduction

Mild cognitive impairment (MCI) and mild Alzheimer's disease (AD) represent critical stages in the cognitive decline continuum, affecting millions of adults worldwide. MCI, characterized by cognitive decline greater than expected for age but not severe enough to interfere significantly with daily functioning, affects approximately 15-20% of adults aged 65 and older [CITATION NEEDED]. Among individuals with MCI, particularly amnestic MCI (aMCI), the annual conversion rate to dementia ranges from 10-15% [CITATION NEEDED]. Mild AD, the earliest symptomatic stage of Alzheimer's disease, represents the progression from MCI to clinically significant cognitive and functional impairment. Current pharmacological interventions for these conditions, including cholinesterase inhibitors and NMDA receptor antagonists, provide modest symptomatic benefits but do not alter disease progression substantially, highlighting an urgent need for novel therapeutic approaches.

Non-invasive brain stimulation (NIBS) techniques have emerged as promising interventions for cognitive enhancement in neurodegenerative conditions. Repetitive transcranial magnetic stimulation (rTMS), including intermittent theta burst stimulation (iTBS) and continuous theta burst stimulation (cTBS), utilizes magnetic fields to modulate neuronal activity in targeted brain regions. Transcranial direct current stimulation (tDCS) applies weak electrical currents to influence neuronal excitability, while transcranial alternating current stimulation (tACS) delivers oscillatory currents at specific frequencies to entrain neural oscillations. The theoretical rationale for NIBS in MCI and mild AD is based on the potential to enhance synaptic plasticity, improve network connectivity, and compensate for neurodegeneration-related dysfunction in memory and cognitive networks, particularly the default mode network and hippocampal-cortical circuits.

Several systematic reviews have examined NIBS interventions in cognitive impairment and dementia, but significant limitations persist in the existing literature. Previous reviews have often combined heterogeneous populations, including moderate to severe dementia alongside MCI, potentially obscuring treatment effects specific to early-stage cognitive decline. Additionally, many reviews have not adequately distinguished between different NIBS modalities or have focused on single techniques, limiting comprehensive evaluation of the evidence base. The rapid evolution of stimulation protocols, including advances in targeting methods and parameter optimization, necessitates updated synthesis of the current evidence.

A critical knowledge gap exists regarding the comparative effectiveness of different NIBS modalities specifically in adults with MCI and mild AD. While individual studies have reported promising results, the optimal stimulation parameters, target populations, and clinical protocols remain unclear. Furthermore, the sustainability of cognitive benefits and potential for disease modification versus symptomatic improvement requires systematic evaluation. The heterogeneity in outcome measures, stimulation protocols, and study designs across trials has hindered definitive conclusions about the therapeutic potential of NIBS in early cognitive decline.

The primary objective of this systematic review and meta-analysis is to evaluate the efficacy and safety of NIBS interventions (rTMS, tDCS, and tACS) compared to sham stimulation, usual care, or active controls in adults with MCI or mild AD. Secondary objectives include examining the comparative effectiveness of different NIBS modalities, identifying optimal stimulation parameters, assessing the durability of treatment effects, and evaluating safety profiles. This review aims to provide evidence-based recommendations for clinical practice and identify priorities for future research in this rapidly evolving field.
