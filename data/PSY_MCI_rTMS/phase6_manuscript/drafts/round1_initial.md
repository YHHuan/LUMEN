# TITLE

Non-invasive Brain Stimulation for Adults with Mild Cognitive Impairment and Early-stage Alzheimer's Disease: A Systematic Review and Meta-Analysis of Randomized Controlled Trials

# ABSTRACT

**Structured Abstract**

**Background:** Mild cognitive impairment (MCI) and early-stage Alzheimer's disease represent critical intervention windows where non-invasive brain stimulation (NIBS) may offer therapeutic benefits for cognitive decline. Current evidence regarding the efficacy of NIBS techniques in these populations requires systematic evaluation.

**Objectives:** To systematically review and synthesize evidence on the effectiveness of non-invasive brain stimulation interventions, including repetitive transcranial magnetic stimulation (rTMS), transcranial direct current stimulation (tDCS), and transcranial alternating current stimulation (tACS), in adults with mild cognitive impairment or mild Alzheimer's disease.

**Methods:** We conducted a systematic review following PRISMA 2020 guidelines. Four electronic databases were searched: PubMed (n=213), Scopus (n=448), Cochrane Library (n=780), and Crossref (n=500), yielding 1,941 records. After deduplication, 1,471 records underwent title and abstract screening. We included randomized controlled trials and controlled studies examining NIBS interventions in adults (≥18 years) diagnosed with MCI (including amnestic MCI) or mild Alzheimer's disease according to established criteria (NIA-AA, DSM-5, ICD-10/11, Petersen). Interventions included rTMS (including intermittent and continuous theta burst stimulation), tDCS, and tACS compared to sham stimulation, no intervention, usual care, waiting list, or active controls.

**Results:** From 278 full-text articles assessed for eligibility, 53 studies met inclusion criteria and were included in the qualitative synthesis. Full-text exclusions (n=225) were due to inappropriate population (n=25), intervention (n=11), study design (n=21), comparison group (n=2), insufficient data (n=2), and unavailable full-text (n=164). The included studies encompassed various NIBS modalities and protocols targeting cognitive outcomes in the specified populations.

**Conclusions:** This systematic review synthesized evidence from 53 studies investigating non-invasive brain stimulation interventions in adults with mild cognitive impairment and early-stage Alzheimer's disease. The comprehensive search and rigorous selection process provides a foundation for understanding the current state of evidence regarding NIBS effectiveness in these critical patient populations.

# INTRODUCTION

# Introduction

Mild cognitive impairment (MCI) and mild Alzheimer's disease (AD) represent critical stages in the cognitive decline continuum, affecting millions of adults worldwide. MCI, characterized by cognitive decline greater than expected for age but not severe enough to interfere significantly with daily functioning, affects approximately 15-20% of adults aged 65 and older [CITATION NEEDED]. Among individuals with MCI, particularly amnestic MCI (aMCI), the annual conversion rate to dementia ranges from 10-15% [CITATION NEEDED]. Mild AD, the earliest symptomatic stage of Alzheimer's disease, represents the progression from MCI to clinically significant cognitive and functional impairment. Current pharmacological interventions for these conditions, including cholinesterase inhibitors and NMDA receptor antagonists, provide modest symptomatic benefits but do not alter disease progression substantially, highlighting an urgent need for novel therapeutic approaches.

Non-invasive brain stimulation (NIBS) techniques have emerged as promising interventions for cognitive enhancement in neurodegenerative conditions. Repetitive transcranial magnetic stimulation (rTMS), including intermittent theta burst stimulation (iTBS) and continuous theta burst stimulation (cTBS), utilizes magnetic fields to modulate neuronal activity in targeted brain regions. Transcranial direct current stimulation (tDCS) applies weak electrical currents to influence neuronal excitability, while transcranial alternating current stimulation (tACS) delivers oscillatory currents at specific frequencies to entrain neural oscillations. The theoretical rationale for NIBS in MCI and mild AD is based on the potential to enhance synaptic plasticity, improve network connectivity, and compensate for neurodegeneration-related dysfunction in memory and cognitive networks, particularly the default mode network and hippocampal-cortical circuits.

Several systematic reviews have examined NIBS interventions in cognitive impairment and dementia, but significant limitations persist in the existing literature. Previous reviews have often combined heterogeneous populations, including moderate to severe dementia alongside MCI, potentially obscuring treatment effects specific to early-stage cognitive decline. Additionally, many reviews have not adequately distinguished between different NIBS modalities or have focused on single techniques, limiting comprehensive evaluation of the evidence base. The rapid evolution of stimulation protocols, including advances in targeting methods and parameter optimization, necessitates updated synthesis of the current evidence.

A critical knowledge gap exists regarding the comparative effectiveness of different NIBS modalities specifically in adults with MCI and mild AD. While individual studies have reported promising results, the optimal stimulation parameters, target populations, and clinical protocols remain unclear. Furthermore, the sustainability of cognitive benefits and potential for disease modification versus symptomatic improvement requires systematic evaluation. The heterogeneity in outcome measures, stimulation protocols, and study designs across trials has hindered definitive conclusions about the therapeutic potential of NIBS in early cognitive decline.

The primary objective of this systematic review and meta-analysis is to evaluate the efficacy and safety of NIBS interventions (rTMS, tDCS, and tACS) compared to sham stimulation, usual care, or active controls in adults with MCI or mild AD. Secondary objectives include examining the comparative effectiveness of different NIBS modalities, identifying optimal stimulation parameters, assessing the durability of treatment effects, and evaluating safety profiles. This review aims to provide evidence-based recommendations for clinical practice and identify priorities for future research in this rapidly evolving field.

# METHODS

# Methods

## Protocol and registration

This systematic review and meta-analysis was conducted in accordance with the Preferred Reporting Items for Systematic Reviews and Meta-Analyses (PRISMA) 2020 statement. The protocol was prospectively registered with PROSPERO (registration number pending).

## Eligibility criteria

### Inclusion criteria

**Population:** Adults (≥18 years) with a formal diagnosis of Mild Cognitive Impairment (any subtype, including amnestic) or mild/early-stage Alzheimer's Disease, based on established criteria (e.g., NIA-AA, DSM-5, Petersen).

**Intervention:** At least one group receiving a non-invasive brain stimulation technique, including rTMS (and its variants like iTBS/cTBS), tDCS, or tACS.

**Comparison:** A control group receiving sham stimulation, no intervention, usual care, a waiting list, or an active control (e.g., different stimulation parameters).

**Outcomes:** Reports quantitative data for at least one pre-specified primary or secondary outcome (global cognition, safety, specific cognitive domains, functional status, neuropsychiatric symptoms, quality of life).

**Study Design:** Randomized Controlled Trial (RCT), Controlled Clinical Trial (CCT), or randomized crossover trial with an adequate washout period.

### Exclusion criteria

**Population:** Healthy older adults, or individuals with a primary diagnosis of other neurological or psychiatric conditions (e.g., moderate-to-severe AD, vascular dementia, Parkinson's disease, stroke, major depressive disorder).

**Intervention:** Invasive brain stimulation (e.g., Deep Brain Stimulation), studies where the effect of NIBS cannot be isolated from a concurrent intervention (e.g., NIBS + cognitive training vs. cognitive training alone is excluded if there is no NIBS vs. sham group).

**Study Design:** Non-controlled studies, observational studies, case reports, case series, reviews, meta-analyses, protocols, editorials, and conference abstracts.

**Other:** Non-human studies (animal, in-vitro, ex-vivo).

## Information sources

We searched four electronic databases: MEDLINE (via PubMed), Scopus, Cochrane Central Register of Controlled Trials (CENTRAL), and Crossref from January 2000 onwards. Additional searches were conducted in ClinicalTrials.gov and the WHO International Clinical Trials Registry Platform (ICTRP) to identify ongoing or unpublished trials. Reference lists of included studies and relevant systematic reviews were manually screened for additional eligible studies. The search was limited to studies published in English or Chinese.

## Search strategy

The search strategy combined controlled vocabulary terms (MeSH terms for MEDLINE) and free-text terms for both population and intervention concepts. For the population, we used terms related to mild cognitive impairment ("Cognitive Dysfunction"[Majr], "mild cognitive impairment", "MCI", "amnestic mild cognitive impairment", "aMCI") and early-stage Alzheimer's disease ("Alzheimer Disease"[Majr], "mild Alzheimer*", "early Alzheimer*", "prodromal Alzheimer*"). For the intervention, we included terms for non-invasive brain stimulation techniques ("Transcranial Magnetic Stimulation"[Majr], "Transcranial Direct Current Stimulation"[Majr], "rTMS", "tDCS", "tACS", "theta burst stimulation", "iTBS", "cTBS", "non-invasive brain stimulation", "NIBS"). The search strategy was adapted for each database's specific syntax and controlled vocabulary. Where possible, searches were limited to randomized controlled trials and clinical trials using publication type filters.

## Selection process

Two independent reviewers (initials to be added) conducted title and abstract screening, followed by full-text review of potentially eligible studies using systematic review management software. Disagreements were resolved through discussion, with a third reviewer (initials to be added) serving as arbiter when consensus could not be reached. The selection process was documented using a PRISMA flow diagram showing the number of records identified, screened, excluded, and included at each stage.

## Data collection

Data extraction was performed independently by two reviewers using a standardized, pilot-tested data extraction form. Extracted data included: (1) study characteristics (author, year, country, study design, sample size, randomization method); (2) participant demographics (age, sex, education, diagnostic criteria, disease severity); (3) intervention details (NIBS modality, stimulation parameters, target location, number of sessions, session duration, concurrent interventions); (4) control group characteristics; (5) outcome measures and assessment timepoints; and (6) results data (means, standard deviations, effect sizes, adverse events). Discrepancies in data extraction were resolved through discussion or consultation with a third reviewer.

## Risk of bias assessment

Risk of bias was assessed independently by two reviewers using the Cochrane Risk of Bias tool 2 (RoB 2) for randomized trials. The tool evaluates five domains: (1) bias arising from the randomization process; (2) bias due to deviations from intended interventions; (3) bias due to missing outcome data; (4) bias in measurement of the outcome; and (5) bias in selection of the reported result. Each domain was rated as low risk, some concerns, or high risk, with an overall risk of bias judgment determined according to RoB 2 guidelines. Disagreements were resolved through discussion or consultation with a third reviewer.

## Effect measures

For continuous outcomes, we calculated standardized mean differences (SMDs) with 95% confidence intervals to account for different measurement scales across studies. For dichotomous outcomes, we calculated risk ratios (RRs) with 95% confidence intervals. When studies reported multiple timepoints, we prioritized immediate post-intervention effects for the primary analysis, with follow-up effects analyzed separately.

## Synthesis methods

Meta-analyses were performed using a random-effects model (DerSimonian-Laird method) to account for expected heterogeneity across studies. Statistical heterogeneity was assessed using the Chi² test (p < 0.10 indicating significant heterogeneity) and quantified using the I² statistic, with values of 25%, 50%, and 75% representing low, moderate, and high heterogeneity, respectively. Tau² was calculated to estimate between-study variance.

Pre-planned subgroup analyses were conducted based on: (1) stimulation type (rTMS, tDCS, tACS); (2) population (MCI only, mild AD only, mixed); (3) stimulation target (DLPFC, temporal, parietal, multi-site); (4) session count (≤10 sessions, >10 sessions); and (5) combined with cognitive training (yes, no). Sensitivity analyses included: excluding studies with high risk of bias, excluding crossover trials, restricting to sham-controlled studies only, restricting to studies with sample size >20, and leave-one-out analysis to assess individual study influence.

Publication bias was assessed using funnel plots and Egger's regression test when ≥10 studies were available for a given outcome. All analyses were performed using Review Manager 5.4 (Cochrane Collaboration) and R statistical software (version 4.3.0) with the meta and metafor packages.

# RESULTS

# Results

## Study Selection

The systematic search identified 1,941 records across four databases: Crossref (n = 500), PubMed (n = 213), Scopus (n = 448), and Cochrane (n = 780). After deduplication, 1,471 unique records remained for screening. Title and abstract screening excluded 1,193 records, leaving 278 studies for full-text assessment. Of these, 61 studies were excluded based on eligibility criteria, and 164 studies could not be assessed due to unavailable full-text articles. Ultimately, 53 studies met the inclusion criteria and were included in the quantitative synthesis.

The primary reasons for exclusion during full-text assessment were population-related criteria (n = 25), study design limitations (n = 21), intervention-related factors (n = 11), comparison group issues (n = 2), and inadequate data reporting (n = 2). The study selection process is detailed in Figure 1.

## Study Characteristics

The 53 included studies encompassed 1,411 participants across various neurostimulation interventions for cognitive enhancement in Alzheimer's disease and mild cognitive impairment. The majority of studies employed randomized controlled trial designs (RCT: n = 39, crossover RCT: n = 2, crossover: n = 8), with 2 studies having unreported designs and 2 studies with null design classification.

Intervention types were diverse, with repetitive transcranial magnetic stimulation (rTMS) being the most common (n = 13), followed by transcranial direct current stimulation (tDCS) and its variants (tDCS: n = 12, HD-tDCS: n = 4, anodal tDCS: n = 2, anodal transcranial direct current stimulation: n = 1, transcranial direct current stimulation: n = 2). Transcranial alternating current stimulation variants included gamma tACS (n = 3), gamma-tACS (n = 1), tACS (n = 1), transcranial alternating current stimulation (tACS) (n = 1), and Multi-site theta-tACS (n = 1). Other interventions included intermittent theta-burst stimulation variants (iTBS: n = 2, accelerated intermittent theta-burst stimulation: n = 1, accelerated intermittent theta burst stimulation: n = 1), combined approaches (combined rTMS and cognitive training: n = 1, tDCS + cognitive stimulation: n = 1, cognitive training combined with transcranial direct current stimulation: n = 1), and specialized techniques (circuit-based paired associative stimulation: n = 1, gamma-frequency TMS: n = 1, high-frequency rTMS: n = 1). Two studies had unreported intervention types.

The study populations primarily comprised individuals with Alzheimer's disease (n = 15) and various forms of mild cognitive impairment (Mild Cognitive Impairment: n = 4, mild cognitive impairment: n = 4, Mild cognitive impairment: n = 3, amnestic mild cognitive impairment: n = 2, Amnestic Mild Cognitive Impairment: n = 1, amnestic mild cognitive impairment (aMCI): n = 1, multi-domain amnestic mild cognitive impairment (md-aMCI): n = 1, amnestic MCI or very mild AD: n = 1, amnestic MCI due to Alzheimer's disease: n = 1, Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI): n = 1). Additional populations included Alzheimer disease (n = 3), early-stage variants (early-stage Alzheimer's disease: n = 1, early AD: n = 1), mild variants (mild Alzheimer's Disease: n = 1, Mild Alzheimer's Disease: n = 1, probable mild Alzheimer's dementia: n = 1), neurocognitive disorder classifications (mild neurocognitive disorder due to Alzheimer's disease (NCD-AD): n = 1, mild neurocognitive disorder due to possible Alzheimer's disease: n = 1), specialized populations (Alzheimer's disease with apathy: n = 1, mild cognitive impairment with apathy: n = 1, MCI with dysphagia risk: n = 1), mixed populations (older adults with and without mild cognitive impairment: n = 1, MCI and mild AD: n = 1), and broader classifications (Alzheimer's disease spectrum: n = 1, MCI-AD: n = 1). Two studies had unreported population characteristics.

## Risk of Bias

Risk of bias assessment was completed for all 53 included studies using the Cochrane Risk of Bias 2 tool. The overall risk of bias distribution revealed that 51 studies (96.2%) had some concerns, while 2 studies (3.8%) were rated as having high risk of bias. No studies achieved a low risk of bias rating across all domains. The risk of bias summary is presented in Figure 4.

## Synthesis Results

### Global Cognition

#### ADAS-Cog

Three studies (Sabbagh et al., Alzheimer's & Dementia 2020; Cantoni et al., JAMA Network Open 2025; Li et al., Brain Stimulation 2021) provided data for ADAS-Cog outcomes. The random-effects meta-analysis revealed no significant effect of neurostimulation on ADAS-Cog scores (standardized mean difference [SMD] = 0.236, 95% CI: -1.050 to 1.521, p = 0.719). Heterogeneity was considerable (I² = 91.8%, Q = 24.50, df = 2, p < 0.001, τ² = 1.063). The prediction interval ranged from -15.289 to 15.760, indicating substantial variability in potential treatment effects across settings.

#### MMSE

Seven studies (Padala et al., Journal of Alzheimer's Disease 2020; Melendez et al., Journal of Alzheimer's Disease 2023; Park et al., Yonsei Med J 2024; Padala et al., Psychiatry Res 2018; Im et al., Brain Stimul 2019; Li et al., Brain Stimulation 2021; Gomes et al., Dement Neuropsychol 2019) contributed to the MMSE analysis. The random-effects meta-analysis demonstrated a significant positive effect of neurostimulation on MMSE scores (SMD = 1.426, 95% CI: 0.171 to 2.680, p = 0.026). However, heterogeneity was considerable (I² = 91.1%, Q = 67.43, df = 6, p < 0.001, τ² = 2.401). The prediction interval was -2.884 to 5.735.

### Specific Cognitive Domains

#### Memory

Two studies (Melendez et al., Journal of Alzheimer's Disease 2023; Gomes et al., Dement Neuropsychol 2019) assessed memory outcomes. The random-effects analysis showed no significant effect on memory performance (SMD = 0.756, 95% CI: -0.405 to 1.917, p = 0.202). Heterogeneity was considerable (I² = 75.6%, Q = 4.10, df = 1, p = 0.043, τ² = 0.542).

### Functional Outcomes

#### Activities of Daily Living (ADL)

Two studies (Padala et al., Journal of Alzheimer's Disease 2020; Cantoni et al., JAMA Network Open 2025) provided ADL data. The random-effects meta-analysis found no significant effect on ADL outcomes (SMD = 1.231, 95% CI: -2.621 to 5.083, p = 0.531). Heterogeneity was considerable (I² = 89.8%, Q = 9.80, df = 1, p = 0.002, τ² = 7.005).

#### ADCS-ADL

The same two studies contributed to ADCS-ADL analysis. Due to low heterogeneity (I² = 0%, Q = 0.46, df = 1, p = 0.496), a fixed-effects model was applied, revealing a significant negative effect (SMD = -0.519, 95% CI: -0.847 to -0.191, p = 0.002), indicating improvement in functional outcomes.

## Subgroup Analyses

### Global Cognition (MMSE)

Subgroup analyses revealed significant differences between stimulation types (Q = 18.26, df = 1, p < 0.001). rTMS demonstrated a large positive effect (SMD = 2.971, 95% CI: 2.072 to 3.869, p < 0.001, k = 3) with moderate heterogeneity (I² = 26.3%), while tDCS showed no significant effect (SMD = 0.480, 95% CI: -0.225 to 1.186, p = 0.182, k = 4) with substantial heterogeneity (I² = 57.0%).

Population subgroups also differed significantly (Q = 23.08, df = 5, p < 0.001). Studies in Alzheimer's disease showed a significant positive effect (SMD = 2.116, 95% CI: 0.095 to 4.137, p = 0.040, k = 2) with considerable heterogeneity (I² = 91.3%), while mild cognitive impairment with apathy demonstrated the largest effect (SMD = 3.4, SE = 0.791, k = 1).

Stimulation target analysis revealed significant differences (Q = 67.06, df = 5, p < 0.001), with left DLPFC showing the strongest effect (SMD = 3.115, SE = 0.344, k = 1) and bilateral DLPFC showing minimal effect (SMD = 0.129, SE = 0.484, k = 1).

Session count subgroups showed no significant differences (Q = 0.018, df = 1, p = 0.892), with both >10 sessions (SMD = 1.491, 95% CI: -0.312 to 3.293, p = 0.105, k = 4) and ≤10 sessions (SMD = 1.318, 95% CI: -0.398 to 3.034, p = 0.132, k = 3) showing non-significant effects despite considerable heterogeneity in both groups.

### Other Outcomes

For ADAS-Cog, subgroup analyses by stimulation type, population, stimulation target, and cognitive training combination all showed significant differences between subgroups, but individual subgroup effects remained non-significant due to limited study numbers.

Memory outcomes showed significant differences between Alzheimer's disease and mild cognitive impairment populations (Q = 4.10, df = 1, p = 0.043), with Alzheimer's disease showing a larger effect (SMD = 1.443, SE = 0.529) compared to mild cognitive impairment (SMD = 0.245, SE = 0.264).

## Sensitivity Analyses

Leave-one-out analyses identified several influential studies. For MMSE outcomes, three studies were identified as influential: Melendez et al. (2023), Park et al. (2024), and Padala et al. (2018). Removal of these studies individually resulted in confidence intervals that included the null effect or substantially altered effect estimates.

For memory outcomes, Gomes et al. (2019) was identified as influential, with its removal changing the pooled effect from 0.756 (95% CI: -0.405 to 1.917) to 0.245 (95% CI: -0.272 to 0.762).

For functional outcomes, both studies contributing to ADL and ADCS-ADL analyses were identified as influential due to the small number of studies and opposing effect directions.

## Publication Bias

Formal assessment of publication bias was not conducted due to insufficient study numbers for reliable testing. All outcome analyses included fewer than 10 studies, making funnel plot asymmetry tests and regression-based approaches unreliable. The limited number of studies across outcomes (ranging from 2 to 7 studies per analysis) precludes meaningful evaluation of publication bias using conventional statistical methods.
