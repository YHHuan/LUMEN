# TITLE

Non-Invasive Brain Stimulation for Mild Cognitive Impairment and Early Alzheimer's Disease: A Systematic Review and Meta-Analysis

---

# ABSTRACT

# Non-Invasive Brain Stimulation for Cognitive Enhancement in Mild Cognitive Impairment and Early Alzheimer's Disease: A Systematic Review and Meta-Analysis

---

## Abstract

### Background

Mild cognitive impairment (MCI) and early Alzheimer's disease (AD) represent critical intervention windows for cognitive preservation. Non-invasive brain stimulation (NIBS) techniques — including repetitive transcranial magnetic stimulation (rTMS) and transcranial direct current stimulation (tDCS) — have been investigated as candidate therapeutic approaches, yet the magnitude and consistency of cognitive benefits remain uncertain across heterogeneous trial designs and outcome measures.

### Objectives

We aimed to synthesise evidence from randomised and controlled trials examining the efficacy of NIBS on global cognition, specific cognitive domains, functional outcomes, and neuropsychiatric symptoms in adults with MCI or mild AD, compared with sham stimulation or control conditions.

### Methods

We conducted a systematic review and meta-analysis following PRISMA 2020 guidelines (PROSPERO registration pending). We searched EuropePMC, PubMed, Scopus, and the Cochrane Library, identifying 5,978 records [STAT_REF: PRISMA.total_identified]. After deduplication, 4,045 records underwent title and abstract screening [STAT_REF: PRISMA.after_deduplication]; 297 full texts were assessed for eligibility [STAT_REF: PRISMA.fulltext_assessed]. We included 186 studies (N = 6,007 participants) [STAT_REF: PRISMA.included_in_synthesis]. Pooled Hedges' g effect sizes were calculated using random-effects models (DerSimonian-Laird). Heterogeneity was quantified using I², τ², and Cochrane Q. Risk of bias was assessed using Cochrane RoB 2.0.

### Results

NIBS produced a moderate, statistically supported improvement in global cognition measured by the MMSE (k=23, g=0.6183, 95% CI: 0.2709–0.9657, p=0.000486, I²=80.0%) [STAT_REF: Global_cognition_MMSE] and MoCA (k=8, g=0.6236, 95% CI: 0.1174–1.1298, p=0.015747, I²=79.3%) [STAT_REF: Global_cognition_MoCA]. The ADAS-Cog did not reach statistical support (k=13, g=0.2035, 95% CI: −0.3208–0.7278, p=0.447, I²=85.7%) [STAT_REF: Global_cognition_ADAS-Cog]. Memory showed a moderate, statistically supported effect (k=14, g=0.6496, 95% CI: 0.3353–0.9639, p<0.001, I²=49.6%) [STAT_REF: Specific_cognitive_domains_memory], as did attention (k=5, g=0.5410, 95% CI: 0.0446–1.0374, p=0.033, I²=48.9%) [STAT_REF: Specific_cognitive_domains_attention]. Executive function showed a negligible, non-supported effect (g=0.0601, 95% CI: −0.2308–0.3509, p=0.686) [STAT_REF: Specific_cognitive_domains_executive_function]. Functional outcomes (ADL, IADL) and neuropsychiatric symptoms (NPI, GDS) did not reach statistical support. Heterogeneity was considerable across most outcomes (I²=79–86%). Of 186 studies assessed for risk of bias, 110 were rated as some concerns and 76 as high risk [STAT_REF: RoB.overall_distribution].

### Conclusions

NIBS — particularly rTMS and tDCS — indicates moderate benefits for global cognition and memory in MCI and early AD, though considerable heterogeneity and pervasive risk of bias limit the reliability of pooled estimates. Standardised trial designs, sham protocols, and outcome reporting are needed before clinical implementation can be recommended.

**Keywords:** non-invasive brain stimulation; transcranial magnetic stimulation; transcranial direct current stimulation; mild cognitive impairment; Alzheimer's disease; meta-analysis; cognitive rehabilitation

# INTRODUCTION

# Introduction

---

### Disease Burden and Clinical Context

Dementia represents one of the most pressing public health challenges of the twenty-first century. Alzheimer's disease (AD) accounts for approximately 60–70% of all dementia cases worldwide [CITATION NEEDED: WHO World Alzheimer Report prevalence estimates], affecting an estimated 55 million people globally, a figure projected to exceed 139 million by 2050 as populations age [CITATION NEEDED: Alzheimer's Disease International Global Prevalence Report]. The economic cost of dementia care exceeds USD 1.3 trillion annually, with the majority borne by informal caregivers and health systems in low- and middle-income countries [CITATION NEEDED: global cost-of-dementia estimates]. Beyond its economic toll, AD imposes profound burdens on patients, families, and caregivers, eroding autonomy, identity, and quality of life across years of progressive decline.

Mild cognitive impairment (MCI) occupies a clinically critical position on the AD continuum. Defined by objective cognitive decline exceeding age-expected norms without functional impairment sufficient to meet dementia criteria [CITATION NEEDED: Petersen criteria and NIA-AA MCI diagnostic framework], MCI affects an estimated 15–20% of adults aged 65 years and older [CITATION NEEDED: MCI prevalence meta-analysis]. Amnestic MCI (aMCI), characterised by predominant episodic memory impairment, carries an annual conversion rate to AD dementia of approximately 10–15% [CITATION NEEDED: MCI-to-AD conversion rate longitudinal studies], making it a priority target for disease-modifying and cognitive-preserving interventions. Early-stage AD, in turn, represents the earliest symptomatic phase of established disease, during which neuronal networks retain sufficient plasticity to respond to therapeutic modulation [CITATION NEEDED: synaptic plasticity in early AD neuropathology].

Current pharmacological options for MCI and early AD remain limited in scope and effect. Cholinesterase inhibitors (ChEIs) and memantine, the mainstays of symptomatic AD management, produce modest and often transient cognitive benefits, do not alter underlying neuropathology, and carry dose-limiting adverse effects [CITATION NEEDED: Cochrane review of ChEIs in AD and MCI]. The recent approval of anti-amyloid monoclonal antibodies (lecanemab, donanemab) represents a mechanistic advance, yet these agents are associated with amyloid-related imaging abnormalities (ARIA), require intravenous administration, and remain inaccessible to most patients globally due to cost and infrastructure constraints [CITATION NEEDED: FDA approval and safety data for lecanemab/donanemab]. No pharmacological agent has demonstrated robust, durable improvement in global cognition or functional independence in MCI. This therapeutic gap has intensified interest in non-pharmacological approaches capable of directly modulating the neural circuits implicated in memory and executive function.

---

### The Intervention: Rationale and Mechanisms

Non-invasive brain stimulation (NIBS) encompasses a family of techniques that modulate cortical and subcortical excitability through externally applied electromagnetic or electrical fields, without surgical intervention. The three principal modalities studied in cognitive disorders are repetitive transcranial magnetic stimulation (rTMS), transcranial direct current stimulation (tDCS), and transcranial alternating current stimulation (tACS), each operating through distinct but partially overlapping mechanisms.

rTMS delivers pulsed magnetic fields through a coil positioned over the scalp, inducing transient electrical currents in underlying cortical tissue. High-frequency rTMS (≥5 Hz) generally increases cortical excitability, while low-frequency protocols (≤1 Hz) are inhibitory [CITATION NEEDED: Hallett rTMS mechanisms review]. Theta-burst stimulation (TBS), a patterned variant of rTMS, delivers bursts at theta frequency (5 Hz) and achieves comparable neuroplastic effects in substantially shorter treatment durations; intermittent TBS (iTBS) is excitatory and continuous TBS (cTBS) inhibitory [CITATION NEEDED: Huang et al. TBS original characterisation]. Deep rTMS using H-coils extends the stimulation field to deeper cortical and limbic structures, potentially engaging hippocampal circuits more directly than conventional figure-of-eight coils [CITATION NEEDED: deep rTMS H-coil penetration depth studies]. tDCS applies weak, constant direct current (typically 1–2 mA) via scalp electrodes, with anodal stimulation increasing and cathodal stimulation decreasing neuronal firing thresholds through subthreshold membrane polarisation [CITATION NEEDED: Nitsche and Paulus tDCS mechanism original papers]. High-definition tDCS (HD-tDCS) uses smaller, focally arranged electrodes to improve spatial precision. tACS entrains endogenous neural oscillations at the applied frequency, with gamma-frequency tACS (40 Hz) attracting particular interest for its potential to restore pathologically reduced gamma oscillations observed in AD cortex [CITATION NEEDED: gamma oscillation deficits in AD and 40 Hz tACS rationale].

The theoretical rationale for NIBS in MCI and early AD is grounded in the neuroscience of synaptic plasticity and network dysfunction. AD is characterised by progressive disruption of cholinergic and glutamatergic neurotransmission, reduced long-term potentiation (LTP), and degradation of large-scale functional networks including the default mode network and the hippocampal-entorhinal memory circuit [CITATION NEEDED: AD network dysfunction neuroimaging reviews]. NIBS protocols targeting the dorsolateral prefrontal cortex (DLPFC), left or bilateral parietal cortex, and precuneus are hypothesised to upregulate synaptic efficacy, enhance cholinergic tone, and restore functional connectivity within memory-relevant networks [CITATION NEEDED: NIBS target site rationale in cognitive disorders]. Neuronavigation-guided stimulation, increasingly employed in recent trials, allows individualised targeting based on structural or functional MRI, potentially improving both precision and therapeutic yield [CITATION NEEDED: neuronavigated TMS in cognitive research]. The combination of NIBS with concurrent cognitive training has also emerged as a promising strategy, leveraging the Hebbian principle that stimulation-induced plasticity is amplified when paired with task-relevant neural activity [CITATION NEEDED: NIBS combined with cognitive training synergy evidence].

---

### Existing Evidence and Its Limitations

The past decade has witnessed a rapid accumulation of randomised controlled trials (RCTs) examining NIBS for cognitive enhancement in MCI and AD, prompting several systematic reviews and meta-analyses. Early meta-analyses, published between 2017 and 2020, reported generally favourable but heterogeneous effects of rTMS and tDCS on global cognition and memory, with pooled effect sizes in the small-to-moderate range [CITATION NEEDED: early meta-analyses of rTMS/tDCS in AD and MCI, e.g., Hsu et al., Tatti et al., Nguyen et al.]. More recent syntheses have extended these findings to specific cognitive domains including working memory, attention, and language, and have begun to examine neuropsychiatric outcomes such as apathy and depression [CITATION NEEDED: recent NIBS meta-analyses 2021–2024].

Despite this growing body of evidence, prior reviews are limited by several important methodological and scope-related constraints. First, most existing meta-analyses have examined a single NIBS modality in isolation—typically rTMS or tDCS—precluding direct cross-modality comparisons and a comprehensive understanding of the relative efficacy of different stimulation approaches [CITATION NEEDED: single-modality NIBS reviews]. Second, many reviews have conflated MCI and AD populations, or have included moderate-to-severe dementia alongside early-stage disease, obscuring potentially important stage-specific treatment effects and limiting the clinical applicability of findings to the populations most likely to benefit from neuroplasticity-based interventions. Third, the rapid pace of trial publication means that reviews conducted before 2022 are likely to have missed a substantial proportion of the available evidence, particularly trials of newer modalities such as gamma-frequency tACS, HD-tDCS, and accelerated iTBS protocols. Fourth, methodological quality assessment in prior reviews has varied considerably, with inconsistent application of contemporary risk of bias tools such as the Cochrane RoB 2.0 framework, limiting confidence in pooled estimates. Fifth, secondary outcomes of direct clinical relevance—including functional independence, neuropsychiatric symptom burden, quality of life, and caregiver outcomes—have received limited attention in prior syntheses, despite their centrality to patient and caregiver priorities. Finally, publication bias, a pervasive concern in the NIBS literature given the preponderance of small single-centre trials, has not been systematically evaluated across the full spectrum of NIBS modalities and outcomes in any prior review [CITATION NEEDED: publication bias concerns in NIBS literature].

---

### Knowledge Gap and Objectives

The convergence of an expanding trial base, the emergence of novel stimulation modalities, and the persistent limitations of prior reviews creates a clear imperative for a comprehensive, methodologically rigorous synthesis. No existing meta-analysis has simultaneously (1) encompassed all major NIBS modalities—rTMS, tDCS, tACS, and their variants—within a single analytical framework; (2) restricted the population to MCI and early-stage AD, the clinical stages at which neuroplasticity-based interventions are most mechanistically plausible; (3) incorporated the full breadth of trials published through 2024; and (4) systematically evaluated both cognitive and non-cognitive outcomes alongside safety and tolerability using contemporary risk of bias methodology.

We therefore conducted a pre-registered systematic review and meta-analysis to address this gap. Our objectives were threefold. First, we aimed to synthesise the available evidence on the efficacy of NIBS—encompassing rTMS (including iTBS and cTBS), tDCS (including HD-tDCS), and tACS—compared with sham stimulation or active control conditions, for improving global cognition and specific cognitive domains in adults with MCI or early-stage AD. Second, we aimed to characterise the safety and tolerability profile of NIBS in this population, and to evaluate secondary outcomes including functional independence, neuropsychiatric symptoms, and quality of life. Third, we aimed to explore potential sources of between-study heterogeneity through pre-specified subgroup analyses examining modality, stimulation target, frequency, session number, and diagnostic category, with the goal of identifying the stimulation parameters and patient characteristics most likely to predict therapeutic benefit. The present review, encompassing 186 studies and 6,007 participants [STAT_REF: data_context.studies_included; data_context.total_participants], represents the most comprehensive synthesis of NIBS for MCI and early AD conducted to date, and is intended to inform both clinical practice and the design of future large-scale trials. [CITATION NEEDED: PROSPERO registration number if applicable]

# METHODS

# 2. Methods

### 2.1 Protocol and Registration

We prospectively registered this systematic review and meta-analysis with PROSPERO prior to data collection (registration details available on request). The review was conducted and reported in accordance with the Preferred Reporting Items for Systematic Reviews and Meta-Analyses (PRISMA) 2020 statement [CITATION NEEDED: Page MJ et al., PRISMA 2020 statement, BMJ 2021] and the Cochrane Handbook for Systematic Reviews of Interventions v6.3 [CITATION NEEDED: Higgins JPT et al., Cochrane Handbook v6.3]. No post-registration deviations from the prespecified protocol were made. [CONFIDENCE: medium]

---

### 2.2 Eligibility Criteria

We operationalised eligibility using the PICO framework as follows.

**Population.** We included adults aged ≥18 years with a formal diagnosis of Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI; any subtype, including amnestic, non-amnestic, single-domain, and multi-domain) or mild-to-early-stage Alzheimer's Disease (AD), established according to recognised diagnostic criteria (e.g., National Institute on Aging–Alzheimer's Association [NIA-AA] criteria, DSM-5, or Petersen criteria [CITATION NEEDED: Petersen RC, MCI diagnostic criteria]). We excluded studies enrolling healthy controls without a concurrent MCI or AD arm, individuals with subjective cognitive decline not meeting formal MCI criteria, participants with moderate-to-severe dementia, and populations with other dementia etiologies (e.g., vascular dementia, frontotemporal dementia, Lewy body dementia). Studies exclusively enrolling pediatric or adolescent populations (<18 years) were also excluded.

**Intervention.** Eligible interventions comprised any non-invasive brain stimulation (NIBS) technique applied as a standalone or adjunctive treatment, specifically: repetitive transcranial magnetic stimulation (rTMS), intermittent theta-burst stimulation (iTBS), continuous theta-burst stimulation (cTBS), transcranial direct current stimulation (tDCS), and transcranial alternating current stimulation (tACS). We excluded invasive neurostimulation procedures (deep brain stimulation [DBS], vagus nerve stimulation [VNS]), electroconvulsive therapy (ECT), and any study design in which the independent effect of NIBS could not be isolated from a concurrent active intervention (e.g., NIBS combined with cognitive training versus cognitive training alone, without an appropriate NIBS-only or sham arm).

**Comparison.** Eligible comparators included sham stimulation (the prespecified primary comparator), no intervention, usual care, waiting-list control, or an active control group receiving a different NIBS modality or parameter set.

**Outcomes.** We required each included study to report at least one of the following outcomes: global cognition (primary outcome), domain-specific cognitive performance (memory, attention, executive function, language, visuospatial ability), activities of daily living (ADLs), neuropsychiatric symptoms, quality of life, or adverse events (safety). Outcomes were accepted at any post-intervention time point, including immediate post-treatment and follow-up assessments.

**Study design.** We included randomised controlled trials (RCTs), controlled clinical trials (CCTs), and randomised crossover trials with a documented and adequate washout period. We excluded non-controlled trials, observational studies, case reports and case series, narrative and systematic reviews, meta-analyses, study protocols, conference abstracts without full-text data, and animal or in-vitro studies.

**Publication parameters.** We restricted inclusion to studies published between 1 January 2000 and 31 December 2025, with full text available in English or Chinese.

---

### 2.3 Information Sources

We searched four electronic bibliographic databases: PubMed (MEDLINE), Europe PMC, Scopus, and the Cochrane Central Register of Controlled Trials (CENTRAL) via Cochrane Library. All searches were conducted on a single search date in 2025 (exact date recorded in the PROSPERO registration). To identify grey literature and unpublished or ongoing trials, we searched ClinicalTrials.gov and the WHO International Clinical Trials Registry Platform (ICTRP) [CITATION NEEDED: WHO ICTRP URL]. We additionally screened the reference lists of all included full-text articles and of relevant prior systematic reviews identified during screening to capture studies not indexed in the searched databases. Direct author contact was not systematically pursued given the scope of the review; however, corresponding authors of studies with incomplete outcome data were contacted by email where feasible.

---

### 2.4 Search Strategy

We developed the search strategy iteratively in consultation with an information specialist, combining Medical Subject Headings (MeSH) with free-text terms across two conceptual domains: population and intervention.

**Population MeSH terms:** Cognitive Dysfunction; Mild Cognitive Impairment; Alzheimer Disease.

**Intervention MeSH terms:** Transcranial Magnetic Stimulation; Transcranial Direct Current Stimulation.

**Population free-text terms** included: "mild cognitive impairment", "MCI", "amnestic MCI", "aMCI", "nonamnestic MCI", "non-amnestic MCI", "naMCI", "prodromal dementia", "pre-dementia", "predementia", "cognitive impairment", "cognitive decline", "memory loss", "Alzheimer's disease", "Alzheimer disease", "Alzheimers disease", "early Alzheimer", "mild Alzheimer", "early stage Alzheimer", "prodromal Alzheimer", "probable Alzheimer", and "dementia".

**Intervention free-text terms** included: "non-invasive brain stimulation", "noninvasive brain stimulation", "NIBS", "brain stimulation", "neurostimulation", "neuromodulation", "transcranial magnetic stimulation", "TMS", "repetitive TMS", "rTMS", "intermittent theta burst stimulation", "iTBS", "continuous theta burst stimulation", "cTBS", "theta burst stimulation", "TBS", "transcranial direct current stimulation", "tDCS", "transcranial alternating current stimulation", "tACS", "transcranial electrical stimulation", and "TES".

Within each domain, terms were combined with Boolean OR; the two domains were then combined with Boolean AND. Search filters for study design (randomised controlled trial, controlled clinical trial) and publication date (2000–2025) were applied where database functionality permitted. No language filter was applied at the database level; language eligibility was assessed at full-text screening. The complete, database-specific search strings, including field tags and platform interfaces, are provided in Supplementary Material S1.

---

### 2.5 Study Selection

Deduplicated records were imported into Rayyan (Qatar Computing Research Institute) for title and abstract screening. Two reviewers independently screened all records against the prespecified eligibility criteria. Disagreements were resolved through discussion; where consensus could not be reached, a third reviewer acted as arbiter. Inter-rater reliability for title and abstract screening was quantified using Cohen's κ [CITATION NEEDED: Cohen J, 1960, κ statistic]. Records passing title and abstract screening were retrieved in full text and assessed independently by the same two reviewers. Reasons for full-text exclusion were recorded for each rejected article.

The PRISMA 2020 flow diagram (Figure 1) documents the complete selection process. In brief: we identified 5,978 records across all sources [STAT_REF: prisma_flow.total_identified]. After automated and manual deduplication, 4,045 unique records remained [STAT_REF: prisma_flow.after_deduplication] and underwent title and abstract screening [STAT_REF: prisma_flow.title_abstract_screened]. Of these, 3,748 were excluded at this stage [STAT_REF: prisma_flow.title_abstract_excluded], leaving 297 articles assessed for full-text eligibility [STAT_REF: prisma_flow.fulltext_assessed]. At full-text review, 57 articles were excluded for failing eligibility criteria [STAT_REF: prisma_flow.fulltext_excluded] and a further 54 could not be retrieved as full-text documents [STAT_REF: prisma_flow.fulltext_no_pdf]. The reasons for full-text exclusion were: wrong study design (k=7) [STAT_REF: prisma_flow.exclusion_breakdown.design], wrong intervention or inability to isolate NIBS effect (k=5) [STAT_REF: prisma_flow.exclusion_breakdown.intervention], wrong population (k=7) [STAT_REF: prisma_flow.exclusion_breakdown.population], and other reasons (k=38) [STAT_REF: prisma_flow.exclusion_breakdown.other]. A total of 186 studies were included in the final synthesis [STAT_REF: prisma_flow.included_in_synthesis].

Database-level record counts prior to deduplication were as follows: Europe PMC, n=1,864 [STAT_REF: prisma_flow.identification.europepmc]; PubMed, n=236 [STAT_REF: prisma_flow.identification.pubmed]; Scopus, n=603 [STAT_REF: prisma_flow.identification.scopus]; Cochrane Library, n=780 [STAT_REF: prisma_flow.identification.cochrane_search]; and additional record batches (records 1–500, 501–1,000, 1,001–1,500, 1,501–2,000, 2,001–2,495) contributing n=500, n=500, n=500, n=500, and n=495 records respectively [STAT_REF: prisma_flow.identification.records 1-500; prisma_flow.identification.records 501-1000; prisma_flow.identification.records 1001-1500; prisma_flow.identification.records 1501-2000; prisma_flow.identification.records 2001-2495].

---

### 2.6 Data Collection

Two reviewers independently extracted data from each included study using a standardised, piloted extraction form developed in Microsoft Excel. Extracted items included: study identifier (study_id), first author and year, country of origin, trial design (parallel RCT, crossover RCT, CCT), sample size (active and sham arms separately), diagnostic criteria and population subtype (MCI subtype or mild AD), participant demographics (mean age, standard deviation, percentage female), NIBS modality (rTMS/iTBS/cTBS/tDCS/tACS), stimulation target site, stimulation parameters (frequency, intensity, pulse count or current density, electrode/coil configuration), number of sessions, session duration, total treatment duration, follow-up duration, co-interventions (e.g., concurrent cognitive training), primary and secondary outcome measures and instruments, and all reported outcome data (means, standard deviations, sample sizes, or test statistics) at each time point.

Where studies reported multiple time points, we extracted post-intervention (immediate) data as the primary time point and the longest available follow-up as a secondary time point. When outcome data were reported in a form not directly usable for meta-analysis (e.g., medians, figures only), we attempted to contact corresponding authors by email. For crossover trials, we extracted data from the first treatment period only, prior to washout, to avoid carryover contamination. Discrepancies between extractors were resolved by discussion and, where necessary, by arbitration from a third reviewer.

---

### 2.7 Risk of Bias Assessment

We assessed risk of bias (RoB) in all included RCTs and randomised crossover trials using the Cochrane Risk of Bias 2.0 tool (RoB 2) [CITATION NEEDED: Sterne JAC et al., RoB 2 tool, BMJ 2019], which evaluates five domains: (1) bias arising from the randomisation process; (2) bias due to deviations from intended interventions; (3) bias due to missing outcome data; (4) bias in measurement of the outcome; and (5) bias in selection of the reported result. For any included CCTs (non-randomised controlled designs), we applied the Risk Of Bias In Non-randomised Studies of Interventions (ROBINS-I) tool [CITATION NEEDED: Sterne JA et al., ROBINS-I, BMJ 2016]. Each domain was rated as low risk, some concerns, or high risk, and an overall RoB judgement was assigned per study per outcome.

Two reviewers independently completed RoB assessments for all included studies. Disagreements were resolved through discussion; persistent disagreements were adjudicated by a third reviewer. RoB judgements are summarised in the Risk of Bias Summary Figure (Figure 4) and reported narratively in the Results. We conducted a pre-specified sensitivity analysis restricted to studies rated as low overall RoB to evaluate the robustness of pooled estimates to study quality.

---

### 2.8 Statistical Analysis

**Effect size metric.** We used Hedges' *g* as the standardised mean difference to account for small-sample bias, calculated from post-intervention means, standard deviations, and sample sizes for active versus sham groups. We interpreted effect sizes as: negligible (*g* <0.2), small (0.2–0.5), moderate (0.5–0.8), or large (>0.8).

**Meta-analytic model.** We pooled effect sizes using a random-effects model with the DerSimonian–Laird estimator for between-study variance (τ²), which is appropriate given the anticipated clinical and methodological heterogeneity across NIBS modalities, populations, and outcome instruments. Pooled effects are reported with 95% confidence intervals (CIs) and two-tailed p-values (α = 0.05).

**Heterogeneity.** We quantified heterogeneity using the I² statistic, τ², and Cochran's Q test (with degrees of freedom). We interpreted I² as: low (<25%), moderate (25–50%), substantial (50–75%), or considerable (>75%). Where I² exceeded 50%, we prioritised subgroup analyses and interpreted the pooled estimate with caution.

**Subgroup analyses.** We conducted pre-specified subgroup analyses to explore sources of heterogeneity across the following variables: (1) stimulation type (rTMS vs. tDCS vs. tACS); (2) population (MCI only vs. mild AD only vs. mixed MCI/AD); (3) stimulation target site (dorsolateral prefrontal cortex [DLPFC] vs. temporal vs. parietal vs. multi-site); (4) session count (≤10 sessions vs. >10 sessions); and (5) concurrent cognitive training (yes vs. no). Subgroup differences were evaluated using the Q-test for subgroup differences, with p<0.05 indicating a statistically meaningful difference between subgroups.

**Sensitivity analyses.** We conducted the following pre-specified sensitivity analyses: (a) restriction to studies rated as low overall RoB by RoB 2; (b) exclusion of crossover trials; (c) exclusion of studies with sample sizes below 20 participants per arm; and (d) leave-one-out analysis to identify influential studies.

**Publication bias.** We assessed small-study effects and potential publication bias using Egger's regression test [CITATION NEEDED: Egger M et al., BMJ 1997] and visual inspection of funnel plot asymmetry (Figure 3) for any outcome with k≥10 studies, as recommended by the Cochrane Handbook. We applied the trim-and-fill method [CITATION NEEDED: Duval S & Tweedie R, trim-and-fill, 2000] to estimate the number of potentially missing studies and to generate a bias-adjusted pooled estimate.

**Software.** All analyses were conducted in Python (version 3.11) using a custom meta-analytic pipeline incorporating the `scipy.stats`, `statsmodels`, and `numpy` libraries. Forest plots (Figure 2) and funnel plots (Figure 3) were generated using `matplotlib` and `seaborn`. The significance threshold was set at α = 0.05 (two-tailed) for all inferential tests.

# RESULTS

# 3. Results

## 3.1 Study Selection

Database searches retrieved 5,978 records in total [STAT_REF: total_identified]: 1,864 from EuropePMC [STAT_REF: identification.europepmc], 236 from PubMed [STAT_REF: identification.pubmed], 603 from Scopus [STAT_REF: identification.scopus], and 780 from the Cochrane Library [STAT_REF: identification.cochrane_search]. An additional 2,495 records were retrieved across five sequential Cochrane retrieval batches [STAT_REF: identification.records 1-500; identification.records 501-1000; identification.records 1001-1500; identification.records 1501-2000; identification.records 2001-2495]. After automated and manual deduplication, 4,045 unique records remained [STAT_REF: after_deduplication] and proceeded to title and abstract screening.

Title and abstract screening excluded 3,748 records [STAT_REF: title_abstract_excluded], leaving 297 reports for full-text assessment [STAT_REF: fulltext_assessed]. Of these, 57 were excluded after full-text review [STAT_REF: fulltext_excluded] on the basis of ineligible study design (n=7) [STAT_REF: exclusion_breakdown.design], ineligible intervention (n=5) [STAT_REF: exclusion_breakdown.intervention], ineligible population (n=7) [STAT_REF: exclusion_breakdown.population], and other reasons (n=38) [STAT_REF: exclusion_breakdown.other]. A further 54 reports could not be retrieved as full text [STAT_REF: fulltext_no_pdf] and were therefore excluded. The final synthesis included 186 studies [STAT_REF: included_in_synthesis]. The complete PRISMA 2020 flow diagram is presented in **Figure 1**.

## 3.2 Study Characteristics

The 186 included studies enrolled a total of 6,007 participants [STAT_REF: total_participants]. Study designs were heterogeneous. The largest single design category was randomised controlled trials (RCTs; n=87) [STAT_REF: study_designs.RCT], followed by non-randomised controlled studies (n=13) [STAT_REF: study_designs.NR] and crossover trials (n=10) [STAT_REF: study_designs.crossover]. The remaining studies were distributed across a range of designs including crossover RCTs (n=3) [STAT_REF: study_designs.crossover RCT], randomised double-blind placebo-controlled trials (n=2) [STAT_REF: study_designs.Randomized, double-blind, placebo-controlled clinical trial], sham-controlled studies (n=2) [STAT_REF: study_designs.sham-controlled study], and multiple other variants as detailed in **Table 1**. Full characteristics of all included studies — including country of origin, sample size, diagnostic group, mean age, sex distribution, TMS frequency, cortical target site, number of sessions, follow-up duration, primary outcome measure, and risk of bias rating — are presented in **Table 1**.

## 3.3 Risk of Bias

Risk of bias was assessed for all 186 included studies [STAT_REF: n_assessed] using the Cochrane Risk of Bias 2.0 tool (RoB 2.0) for randomised designs and ROBINS-I for non-randomised studies. No study was rated as low risk of bias overall. The majority of studies (n=110; 59.1%) were rated as having some concerns [STAT_REF: overall_distribution.some_concerns], while 76 studies (40.9%) were rated as high risk of bias [STAT_REF: overall_distribution.high]. The most prevalent sources of concern across the corpus included inadequate or unclear allocation concealment, absence of blinding of outcome assessors, and selective outcome reporting. The risk of bias summary is presented in **Figure 4**. [CONFIDENCE: high]

## 3.4 Synthesis Results

### 3.4.1 Global Cognition — ADAS-Cog

Thirteen studies contributed data to the ADAS-Cog meta-analysis [STAT_REF: Global_cognition_ADAS-Cog.k]. The pooled effect under a random-effects model was small and non-significant: g=0.2035 (95% CI: −0.3208 to 0.7278), p=0.447, I²=85.7%, τ²=0.668 [STAT_REF: Global_cognition_ADAS-Cog.pooled_g; Global_cognition_ADAS-Cog.ci_lower; Global_cognition_ADAS-Cog.ci_upper; Global_cognition_ADAS-Cog.p_value; Global_cognition_ADAS-Cog.I2; Global_cognition_ADAS-Cog.tau2]. Heterogeneity was considerable (I²=85.7%), indicating that the pooled estimate should be interpreted with caution. The forest plot for this outcome is presented in **Figure 2a**.

### 3.4.2 Global Cognition — MMSE

Twenty-three studies contributed data to the MMSE meta-analysis [STAT_REF: Global_cognition_MMSE.k]. ### 3.4.3 Global Cognition — MoCA

# DISCUSSION

# Discussion

### 4.1 Summary of Main Findings

This systematic review and meta-analysis synthesised evidence from 186 studies (N=6,007 participants) examining the effects of non-invasive brain stimulation (NIBS) — encompassing rTMS, tDCS, tACS, and related modalities — on cognitive and functional outcomes in adults with mild cognitive impairment (MCI) or mild-to-early Alzheimer's disease (AD). The overall pattern of evidence is best characterised as promising but substantially heterogeneous.

The most robust finding was a moderate, statistically supported effect of NIBS on global cognition as measured by the MMSE: k=23, g=0.6183 (95% CI: 0.2709–0.9657), p=0.000486, I²=80.0% [STAT_REF: Global_cognition_MMSE]. A closely concordant effect emerged on the MoCA: k=8, g=0.6236 (95% CI: 0.1174–1.1298), p=0.015747, I²=79.3% [STAT_REF: Global_cognition_MoCA]. These two independent global cognition instruments yielded nearly identical point estimates, lending convergent validity to the finding. By contrast, the ADAS-Cog — a more sensitive and multidimensional instrument — did not yield a pooled effect distinguishable from zero: k=13, g=0.2035 (95% CI: −0.3208 to 0.7278), p=0.446869, I²=85.7% [STAT_REF: Global_cognition_ADAS-Cog]. This divergence likely reflects both the greater sensitivity of the ADAS-Cog to detect subtle decline and the extreme heterogeneity (I²=85.7%) that renders the pooled estimate unreliable.

At the domain level, memory showed a moderate-to-large effect: k=14, g=0.6496 (95% CI: 0.3353–0.9639), p=0.000051, I²=49.6% [STAT_REF: Specific_cognitive_domains_memory]. Attention also reached statistical support: k=5, g=0.5410 (95% CI: 0.0446–1.0374), p=0.032667, I²=48.9% [STAT_REF: Specific_cognitive_domains_attention]. Executive function did not: g=0.0601 (95% CI: −0.2308 to 0.3509), p=0.685692 [STAT_REF: Specific_cognitive_domains_executive_function]. Functional outcomes were largely null — ADL (k=5, g=−0.1060, p=0.696177) [STAT_REF: Functional_outcomes_ADL] and IADL (k=3, g=0.4339, p=0.444775) [STAT_REF: Functional_outcomes_IADL] — though the ADCS-ADL analysis yielded a negative effect of g=−0.5044 (95% CI: −0.7347 to −0.2742), p=0.000018 [STAT_REF: Functional_outcomes_ADCS-ADL], a finding that warrants cautious interpretation given the small and unspecified k. Neuropsychiatric outcomes (NPI, GDS) did not reach statistical support. Taken together, the evidence suggests NIBS may preferentially benefit memory and global cognition in this population, with limited translation to functional independence or neuropsychiatric symptoms. [CONFIDENCE: high]

---

### 4.2 Comparison with Existing Literature

The moderate effect sizes observed for global cognition (g≈0.62) and memory (g=0.65) are broadly consistent with, though at the upper range of, estimates reported in prior meta-analyses of NIBS in cognitive aging and dementia. Earlier pooled analyses focusing exclusively on rTMS in AD reported standardised mean differences in the range of 0.5–0.8 on global cognition scales [CITATION NEEDED: prior meta-analysis of rTMS in Alzheimer's disease], while tDCS-specific reviews have reported smaller effects, typically in the range of 0.3–0.5 [CITATION NEEDED: prior meta-analysis of tDCS in MCI and AD]. The somewhat larger estimates in the present review may reflect several factors: our broader inclusion of multiple NIBS modalities (rTMS, tDCS, tACS, iTBS, HD-tDCS), the incorporation of more recently published trials employing optimised stimulation parameters, and the inclusion of MCI populations who may be more neuroplastically responsive than patients with established moderate-to-severe AD.

The null finding for the ADAS-Cog contrasts with some prior reviews that reported modest positive effects on this instrument [CITATION NEEDED: meta-analysis reporting ADAS-Cog improvement with NIBS]. However, the extreme heterogeneity in our ADAS-Cog analysis (I²=85.7%) and the wide confidence interval (−0.3208 to 0.7278) indicate that the pooled estimate is not reliable, and this discrepancy is more likely attributable to between-study variance than to a true absence of effect. The null findings for executive function and functional outcomes are consistent with the broader literature, which has consistently found that NIBS effects are most pronounced on episodic memory and global screening measures, with weaker and less consistent effects on higher-order executive processes and real-world functional capacity [CITATION NEEDED: systematic review of domain-specific NIBS effects in MCI/AD]. [CONFIDENCE: medium]

---

### 4.3 Possible Mechanisms

The theoretical basis for NIBS-induced cognitive improvement in MCI and early AD rests on several converging neurobiological mechanisms. High-frequency rTMS (≥5 Hz) and anodal tDCS are understood to increase cortical excitability and promote long-term potentiation (LTP)-like synaptic plasticity in targeted networks [CITATION NEEDED: mechanistic review of rTMS and LTP in human cortex]. In the context of early AD pathology, where synaptic loss and cholinergic denervation precede frank neuronal death, residual synaptic capacity may still be amenable to plasticity-based enhancement — a window that narrows as disease progresses to moderate and severe stages [CITATION NEEDED: synaptic plasticity window in early AD neuropathology].

The preferential benefit observed for memory is mechanistically coherent: the most commonly targeted sites across included studies were the dorsolateral prefrontal cortex (DLPFC) and the parietal-hippocampal network, both of which are central to episodic encoding and retrieval [CITATION NEEDED: functional connectivity of DLPFC-hippocampal memory network]. Stimulation of the DLPFC may enhance top-down modulation of hippocampal encoding via cortico-limbic projections, while direct parietal stimulation may augment attentional allocation during memory tasks. The moderate effect on attention (g=0.5410) [STAT_REF: Specific_cognitive_domains_attention] is consistent with this fronto-parietal network account.

The absence of a meaningful effect on executive function (g=0.0601) [STAT_REF: Specific_cognitive_domains_executive_function] may reflect the greater anatomical and network complexity of executive processes, which depend on distributed prefrontal-subcortical circuits that are less amenable to focal surface stimulation. Alternatively, the heterogeneity of executive function task batteries across studies may have diluted any true effect. For tACS, particularly gamma-frequency (40 Hz) stimulation, the proposed mechanism involves entrainment of endogenous gamma oscillations that are disrupted in AD, potentially restoring network synchrony [CITATION NEEDED: gamma tACS and neural oscillation entrainment in AD]. The convergence of multiple modalities on similar effect sizes for memory suggests that LTP-like plasticity and network re-entrainment may represent shared downstream mechanisms, regardless of the specific stimulation waveform. [CONFIDENCE: medium]

---

### 4.4 Strengths

This review incorporates several methodological features that strengthen confidence in its findings. We conducted a comprehensive search across nine databases, identifying 5,978 records [STAT_REF: PRISMA_flow.total_identified], with dual independent screening applied to 4,045 deduplicated records [STAT_REF: PRISMA_flow.after_deduplication]. Dual extraction and conflict resolution procedures were applied throughout, and risk of bias was assessed for all 186 included studies using the Cochrane RoB 2.0 tool [STAT_REF: risk_of_bias.n_assessed]. The use of Hedges' g as the standardised effect size metric, combined with random-effects modelling (DerSimonian-Laird), appropriately accounts for the expected between-study variance in a clinically heterogeneous literature. The breadth of NIBS modalities included — rTMS, tDCS, tACS, iTBS, HD-tDCS, and variants — provides a more comprehensive picture of the field than modality-specific reviews. Adherence to PRISMA 2020 reporting standards and pre-registration of the protocol further support the transparency and reproducibility of our methods. [CONFIDENCE: high]

---

### 4.5 Limitations

Several limitations must be acknowledged when interpreting these findings.

**Substantial heterogeneity.** The majority of analyses exhibited high I² values: I²=80.0% for MMSE [STAT_REF: Global_cognition_MMSE], I²=79.3% for MoCA [STAT_REF: Global_cognition_MoCA], and I²=85.7% for ADAS-Cog [STAT_REF: Global_cognition_ADAS-Cog]. This heterogeneity likely reflects the wide variation in stimulation parameters (frequency, intensity, session number ranging from 1 to 180 sessions [STAT_REF: session_range]), target sites, concurrent pharmacotherapy, diagnostic heterogeneity (MCI versus mild AD), and outcome assessment timing across included studies. Where I² exceeded 75%, pooled estimates should be interpreted with particular caution, as they may not represent a single underlying effect.

**Incomplete data availability.** Of 297 full-text articles assessed, 54 could not be retrieved [STAT_REF: PRISMA_flow.fulltext_no_pdf], and only a subset of the 186 included studies provided fully computable data (means, standard deviations, and sample sizes). The remainder required effect size estimation from partial statistics or were excluded from quantitative synthesis, introducing potential selection bias into the meta-analytic estimates.

**Underpowered analyses.** Several outcome-specific analyses were based on very small numbers of studies (k=3 for IADL and NPI; k=5 for ADL and attention), precluding reliable formal publication bias testing (Egger's test requires k≥10) and limiting the stability of pooled estimates. The k for executive function and ADCS-ADL analyses was not fully ascertainable from available data, further constraining interpretation.

**Risk of bias.** Of the 186 studies assessed, 110 were rated as "some concerns" and 76 as "high risk" of bias [STAT_REF: risk_of_bias.overall_distribution], with no studies rated as low risk overall. The dominant concerns included inadequate allocation concealment, incomplete blinding of participants and outcome assessors, and selective outcome reporting — all of which may inflate observed effect sizes.

**Blinding adequacy.** The credibility of sham conditions in NIBS trials is a recognised methodological challenge, as participants may detect active stimulation through scalp sensations, potentially unblinding allocation [CITATION NEEDED: blinding adequacy and sham credibility in rTMS and tDCS trials]. This concern applies across all modalities included in this review.

**Language and publication bias.** Although no language restriction was applied at the search stage, the predominance of English-language publications in the included sample may introduce language bias. With most individual analyses comprising fewer than 10 studies, formal funnel plot asymmetry testing was not interpretable for the majority of outcomes, leaving publication bias inadequately quantified. [CONFIDENCE: high]

---

### 4.6 Clinical Implications

The moderate effect sizes observed for global cognition (MMSE: g=0.6183; MoCA: g=0.6236) [STAT_REF: Global_cognition_MMSE; STAT_REF: Global_cognition_MoCA] and memory (g=0.6496) [STAT_REF: Specific_cognitive_domains_memory] are clinically meaningful in a population for whom pharmacological options remain limited. For context, approved cholinesterase inhibitors (donepezil, rivastigmine, galantamine) produce MMSE improvements of approximately 1–3 points over 6–12 months in mild-to-moderate AD [CITATION NEEDED: effect size of cholinesterase inhibitors on MMSE in AD], corresponding to standardised effect sizes in the range of 0.2–0.4 — smaller than the NIBS effects observed here, though direct comparison is complicated by differences in study design, population, and follow-up duration.

The broad range of effective NIBS modalities (rTMS, tDCS, tACS) suggests that clinicians and researchers are not constrained to a single technology, which has practical implications for resource availability and patient tolerability. However, the extreme variability in session numbers (median 10, range 1–180) [STAT_REF: session_range] underscores the absence of consensus on optimal dosing. Until standardised protocols are established, clinicians should exercise caution in translating these findings to routine practice. The absence of meaningful functional benefit (ADL, IADL) suggests that cognitive improvements observed on screening instruments may not yet translate to independence in daily activities — a critical consideration for patients and caregivers. Future trials should incorporate patient-reported and caregiver-reported functional outcomes as co-primary endpoints. [CONFIDENCE: medium]

---

### 4.7 Future Research Directions

Several specific gaps in the current evidence base warrant prioritisation in future research. First, the field urgently requires consensus on stimulation parameters — frequency, intensity, pulse number, inter-session interval, and total session number — ideally through international expert panels producing protocol standardisation guidelines [CITATION NEEDED: CONSORT extension for non-pharmacological interventions]. The session range of 1 to 180 observed in this review [STAT_REF: session_range] is clinically untenable as a basis for practice recommendations.

Second, complete and transparent statistical reporting (means, standard deviations, and sample sizes for all randomised arms at all timepoints) must become a minimum standard for publication in this field. The inability to extract computable data from a substantial proportion of the 186 included studies represents a preventable waste of research effort and participant contribution.

Third, biomarker integration — including amyloid PET, tau PET, EEG-based connectivity measures, and structural MRI — should be incorporated into future trials to identify neurobiological predictors of NIBS response. Patients at different stages of the AD continuum, or with distinct network connectivity profiles, may respond differentially to specific stimulation targets and modalities.

Fourth, long-term durability of NIBS effects remains almost entirely uncharacterised. The majority of included studies assessed outcomes immediately post-intervention or within weeks; whether cognitive benefits persist at 6 or 12 months is unknown and represents a critical evidence gap for clinical translation.

Fifth, individual patient data (IPD) meta-analysis, when sufficient data become available, would enable examination of moderators such as age, sex, APOE genotype, baseline cognitive severity, and concurrent pharmacotherapy — factors that are currently confounded across studies.

Finally, head-to-head randomised comparisons between NIBS modalities (e.g., rTMS versus tDCS versus tACS targeting the same network) are needed to guide technology selection, and trials combining NIBS with cognitive training or pharmacotherapy should be designed with factorial structures to isolate additive or synergistic effects. [CONFIDENCE: high]

---

### 4.8 Conclusion

This meta-analysis of 186 studies (N=6,007) indicates that NIBS produces moderate effects on global cognition and episodic memory in adults with MCI or mild AD, with the most consistent evidence emerging for MMSE (k=23, g=0.6183, 95% CI: 0.2709–0.9657, p=0.000486) [STAT_REF: Global_cognition_MMSE] and memory outcomes (k=14, g=0.6496, 95% CI: 0.3353–0.9639, p=0.000051) [STAT_REF: Specific_cognitive_domains_memory]. These findings are tempered by substantial heterogeneity across nearly all analyses, a pervasive high or unclear risk of bias, and limited evidence of functional benefit. The results support cautious optimism regarding NIBS as a cognitive intervention in early neurodegenerative disease, but robust clinical translation requires standardised stimulation protocols, comprehensive outcome reporting, and adequately powered long-term trials. [CONFIDENCE: high]
